# Slot machine payouts

I started by asking the shell for the real timestamp, because the filename matters more than my memory of the clock. It came back `2026-04-27_2024`, so this entry gets that stamp. I also checked the working tree before touching anything. There were already modified docs, a deleted outbox file, `Clean up.md`, `decisions/`, and three earlier journal entries. I am leaving all of that alone and adding only this file.

I did not want to drift back into the places this repo has been pulling attention toward lately: Boss AI, Dragon combat, map topology, audio, link cable, and Pokegear radio. I asked `rg` for slot, Game Corner, coin, reel, payout, and card-flip names under `engine`, `data`, `constants`, `ram`, and `maps`. The result immediately had the shape of a little machine room: `engine/games/slot_machine.asm`, `engine/games/card_flip.asm`, `ram/wram.asm`, `engine/events/specials.asm`, `data/events/special_pointers.asm`, the Goldenrod and Celadon Game Corner maps, and a handful of sprite animation tables. I am choosing the slot machine, not card flip, because the search results showed comments about bias, manipulation, payouts, Golem, Chansey, and "exciting reel #3 modes with no success hope." That sounds like code with a personality.

I opened `data/events/special_pointers.asm` and `engine/events/specials.asm` first because I wanted the smallest doorway into the feature. The special pointer table has `SlotMachine` and `CardFlip` next to each other, and `engine/events/specials.asm` keeps them almost identical: check the Coin Case and coins, choose the farcall bank and label, then `StartGameCornerGame`. That made me click away quickly. The wrapper is clean and boring; the interesting thing must be whatever state the map scripts pass in before the special runs.

I opened `maps/GoldenrodGameCorner.asm` and `maps/CeladonGameCorner.asm` around their machine scripts. Goldenrod has `GoldenrodGameCornerSlotsMachineScript`, which does `random 6`, jumps to the lucky script only on zero, otherwise `setval FALSE` and calls `special SlotMachine`. The lucky script sets `TRUE`. Celadon is the same idea with slightly different naming: `CeladonGameCornerLuckySlotMachineScript` sometimes falls through to the normal script, and the normal script sets `TRUE` while the lucky branch sets `FALSE`. That inversion made me stop. I do not know yet whether `TRUE` means "normal" or "lucky"; the labels disagree across towns unless the random branch direction is doing a little joke. I left the maps because the next question is not where the machines are, but what `wScriptVar` means inside the engine.

I opened the top of `engine/games/slot_machine.asm`. It starts with the symbol values packed four apart: seven, Pokeball, cherry, Pikachu, Squirtle, Staryu, with `SLOTS_NO_MATCH` as `-1`. Then there is a whole jumptable vocabulary before `_SlotMachine` even starts: wait for each reel, flash if win, give earned coins, payout text and animation, quit. The reel actions are more dramatic than a menu: normal speed, half speed, quarter speed, skip reel two to 7, fast-spin reel two until lined-up 7s, drop reel, Golem, Chansey, egg. This is not just random icons stopping; it is a staged show.

Still in the top of `slot_machine.asm`, `_SlotMachine` turns off text scroll, loads graphics, loops `SlotsLoop`, plays the quit SFX, clears palettes, restores sprite size, and returns. The init routine clears `wSlots`, loads three slot graphics blobs and the tilemap, sets 8x16 sprites, clears the broader slot WRAM range, initializes reel tiles and palettes, installs the slots sprite animation dictionary, sets the jumptable to `SLOTS_INIT`, and initializes `wSlotBias` to `SLOTS_NO_BIAS`. At the end it plays Game Corner music and sets `wKeepSevenBiasChance` with a comment that says `87.5% chance`, unless a masked random result makes it `TRUE` for `12.5% chance`. I clicked away from the top because two pieces of state now mattered: the map's `setval`, and this separate keep-seven-bias flag. I need the WRAM shape before the control flow makes sense.

I opened `ram/wram.asm` around the slot block. `wSlots` begins with three `slot_reel` structures, then three stopped-reel snapshots, then `wSlotBias`, `wSlotBet`, `wFirstTwoReelsMatching`, `wFirstTwoReelsMatchingSevens`, `wSlotMatched`, `wPayout`, current reel coordinates, and `wSlotBuildingMatch`. The delay byte is somewhere else, in a later WRAM union block, and `wKeepSevenBiasChance` shares a miscellaneous byte with `wPokeFluteCuredSleep` and `wTempRestorePPItem`. That last alias pileup is classic WRAM pressure: the byte is not morally a slot-machine byte, it is just available during the minigame. I clicked away because the names told me the control flow probably computes "building match" after reel two and "matched" after reel three.

I opened `engine/events/specials.asm` again around `StartGameCornerGame` because I wanted to know whether `wScriptVar` survives the menu transition. It does: `StartGameCornerGame` queues the far script, fades to menu, pulls the queued bank and pointer back out, farcalls it, exits menus, and returns. It does not clear `wScriptVar`. That made the map `setval` real evidence, not dead preamble.

I jumped to `Slots_InitBias` because the `wScriptVar` search pointed straight at it. It first checks `wSlotBias` and immediately returns if the bias is zero. That means a seven bias can persist across a winning payout, because `SLOTS_SEVEN` is zero and this routine treats zero as "already settled; keep it." If the bias is not zero, it chooses `.Normal` unless `wScriptVar` is nonzero, in which case it chooses `.Lucky`. The tables are thresholds from a random byte to a desired symbol or no bias. Normal barely touches sevens and pushes only up to 19 percent before falling to no bias. Lucky puts more mass on cherry, less on some other symbols, and still leaves most spins unbiased. This answers the map question: `TRUE` means use the Lucky table. Goldenrod's one explicitly lucky machine is always lucky, while its ordinary machines have a one-in-six chance to become lucky for that play; Celadon names its first script "Lucky" but five-sixths of those machines set `FALSE`, while one-sixth routes to the normal script that sets `TRUE`. I do not know whether that is inherited quirk or a labeling mistake, but I am not fixing anything in a journal.

I opened the main jumptable body from `SlotsAction_Init` through payout. The spine is tidy. Init clears the matching flags and sets `wSlotMatched` to no match. Bet-and-start asks the player for 1, 2, or 3 coins, illuminates the corresponding line lights, calls `Slots_InitBias`, starts all reels at normal rate, and gives each reel a manipulation counter of four. Then the code waits for A three times, delegates each stop to `Slots_StopReel1`, `Slots_StopReel2`, and `Slots_StopReel3`, stores each stopped three-icon state, flashes if `wSlotMatched` is not no-match, calculates payout, prints result text and animation, increments the coin case one coin at a time until `wPayout` drains, and asks to play again. I clicked away from this section because the payout path is almost disappointingly honest: table lookup, coin drip, max-coin guard. The code with opinions lives in the stop routines.

I opened `Slots_GetPayout` because the filename slug I chose says payouts and I wanted the literal table. Seven pays 300, Pokeball 50, cherry 6, Pikachu 8, Squirtle 10, Staryu 15. The table index is a little cute: `wSlotMatched` stores symbols four apart, but payout does `srl a`, then adds that offset into a word table. Because the symbol constants are 0, 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, shifting once gives byte offsets 0, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10. It is not converting to an ordinal; it is converting to a word-table byte offset. I lingered there because that is a compact old-assembly trick that would be easy to "simplify" wrong.

Right below the payout text, I found the comment that confirmed the earlier smell: after lined-up sevens, `wKeepSevenBiasChance = 1` is described as the rare mode with worse odds to favor seven streaks, 12.5 percent versus 25 percent, and the comment says maybe either the initialization or this code meant the percentages to be flipped. That is exactly the kind of small inherited uncertainty I like seeing documented near the code instead of buried in a wiki. I left that block because I now understood the seven-streak state: sevens can keep `wSlotBias` at zero for another round, but only sometimes.

I opened the reel-action block around `ReelAction_StopReel1`, `ReelAction_StopReel2`, and `ReelAction_StopReel3`. Reel one is permissive: if biased, it can manipulate up to four slots and stops early if the biased symbol appears anywhere in the three visible positions, even on a line the current bet cannot use. Reel two is stricter: it checks whether the first two reels have a usable line match for the current bet before it stops. Reel three is the final judge: if a match is present but not the biased symbol, it keeps spending its four-step manipulation budget; if there is no match and no bias, it stops. This is why the comments feel more like stage direction than math. The player presses A, but the machine has a few frames of editorial control after each press.

I kept reading into the special reel-two skip-to-seven action because its comment was too good to leave. The code says it ensures seven symbols become lined up in the first two reels, but "more often than not" it only gets hopes up by making exciting reel-three modes with no success hope more common. That is blunt. The setup freezes reel two, waits, plays the ball-throw sound, fast-spins reel two, then stops only once `Slots_CheckMatchedFirstTwoReels` says the sevens are lined up. This is not a payout mechanism; it is suspense machinery.

I opened the Golem and Chansey sections next because those names were the theatrical part of the reel-action constants. Golem can force sevens if the bias is seven, or force nothing if there is no bias, by throwing Golem until the desired result appears. Chansey is rarer and only possible with seven bias; it spawns Chansey, waits for the egg, then drops reel three by seventeen slots at a time until seven appears. The comments are unusually clear about what each spectacle guarantees. Golem can be fake drama or real help depending on bias. Chansey is the honest jackpot helper.

I opened `Slots_CheckMatchedFirstTwoReels` and `Slots_CheckMatchedAllThreeReels` after that, because the stop routines depend on their carry flag and result byte. Both routines dispatch based on the current bet: one coin checks only the middle row, two coins add top and bottom, three coins add both diagonals. The two-reel checker compares reel one against the current reel state, stores `wSlotBuildingMatch`, and separately marks whether that partial match is sevens. The three-reel checker compares reel one, current reel three, and the saved reel two state, then stores `wSlotMatched`. I noticed the row naming is slightly inverted by screen orientation: bottom row looks at offset zero, top row at offset two. That is not wrong, but it is the kind of thing I would want to know before touching this file.

I opened the reel tilemaps near the bottom of `slot_machine.asm` because I wanted to see whether the reels were symmetric or tuned. They are tuned. Reel one has two sevens in the fifteen-symbol loop, reel two and three have one each, and the first three positions are repeated after the loop so copying the visible window does not need bounds checks. That repetition is a quiet little optimization: instead of branching around the end of the reel, the table physically includes the wraparound view.

I opened `Slots_SpinReels` and `Slots_UpdateReelPositionAndOAM` because the tilemap repetition made me wonder where the visible window is copied. Each frame spins all three reel structs. If the spin distance crosses a sixteen-pixel boundary, it reloads OAM from the reel tilemap and advances `REEL_POSITION`, wrapping at `REEL_SIZE`. Between full-symbol steps, it just nudges the OAM y positions by the spin rate. The OAM loader writes two sprites per symbol row and derives attributes by shifting the tile id, then setting priority. There is even an unreferenced `GetUnknownSlotReelData` comment saying the final OAM path reuses tile IDs as palettes. I clicked away because this answered the physical question: the reels are not redrawn as a tilemap every frame; their visible strips are sprite-driven.

I opened the sprite animation tables because Golem and Chansey had crossed into the general sprite animation engine. `engine/sprite_anims/functions.asm` has `AnimSeq_SlotsGolem`, `AnimSeq_SlotsChansey`, and `AnimSeq_SlotsChanseyEgg` entries that call back into `Slots_AnimateGolem` and `Slots_AnimateChansey`. The object table binds `SPRITE_ANIM_OBJ_SLOTS_GOLEM`, `SPRITE_ANIM_OBJ_SLOTS_CHANSEY`, and `SPRITE_ANIM_OBJ_SLOTS_EGG` to the slots dictionary, and the framesets show Golem flipping through four frames while Chansey swaps into a second frameset before ending. I did not follow every OAM byte. The point was just to confirm this slot machine owns a real little animation stack, not just reel glyphs.

I opened the Game Corner NPC text last, because after seeing how the code manipulates luck I wanted to know whether the map tells the player anything honest. Goldenrod has an NPC saying one machine pays out more than the others, another saying the new slot machine is more of a challenge than Celadon's, and a card-flip fan saying card flip is easier to figure out but pays lower. Celadon has the direct line: "The odds vary among machines." That made the `setval` weirdness feel less like hidden cruelty and more like intended texture. The player is not given the table, but the world tells them the machines differ.

I briefly opened the top of `engine/games/card_flip.asm` because it kept appearing beside slots in every doorway. It starts with a comment that graphics for an unused Game Corner game were meant to be there, then `_CardFlip` does the same no-text-scroll, palette, tilemap, sprite clear, and LCD setup pattern. I clicked away after the first screenful. Card flip is its own room, and this entry had already found the slot machine's moving parts.

I am stopping here because the next honest step would be probabilistic: simulate or compute expected value by machine type, bet size, bias state, and reel-action intervention. That would be a different artifact. For this walk, the useful thing is the shape: map scripts choose normal versus lucky tables through `wScriptVar`; `Slots_InitBias` picks a desired symbol or no bias; reel stop actions spend small manipulation budgets after player input; special Golem and Chansey modes create suspense and sometimes force outcomes; payout itself is just a small table and a coin-drip animation.
