# Review: smogtours-gen2ou-930759

Replay: https://replay.pokemonshowdown.com/smogtours-gen2ou-930759

Tournament context:
https://www.smogon.com/forums/threads/gsc-ou-winter-seasonal-8-round-13-losers-bracket-replays-required.3781520/

Format: vanilla GSC OU. This is useful for strategic study, not romhack
mechanics proof.

Length: 53 turns.

Players: underlying defeated RUBYBLOOD.

## Team Shapes

underlying revealed:

- Exeggutor: Thief, Psychic.
- Cloyster: Toxic, Spikes.
- Snorlax: Double-Edge.
- Gengar: Thunderbolt, Dynamic Punch, Ice Punch, Explosion.
- Zapdos: Reflect, Thunderbolt, Hidden Power.
- Tyranitar: Crunch, Rock Slide, Surf.

RUBYBLOOD revealed:

- Snorlax: Curse, Double-Edge, Rest, Sleep Talk.
- Zapdos: Thunder.
- Tyranitar: Crunch, Rock Slide.
- Forretress: Rapid Spin, Spikes.
- Alakazam: Toxic, Recover.
- Steelix: Roar, Earthquake, Explosion.

## Phase Map

Opening, turns 1-8:

- underlying's Exeggutor steals Zapdos's Leftovers on turn 1, but the immediate
  route becomes Cloyster against Snorlax.
- Cloyster poisons Curse Snorlax on turn 4 and sets Spikes on turn 5 before
  dying to a critical-hit Double-Edge.
- This is a material loss but not empty material. Snorlax is poisoned, Spikes
  are up, and RUBYBLOOD's grounded switches are now paying a route tax.

Hazard and spin fight, turns 9-16:

- RUBYBLOOD sends Forretress into Snorlax on turn 9.
- underlying switches Gengar into Rapid Spin on turn 10, blocking the first
  removal attempt.
- RUBYBLOOD later gets Spikes up on turn 15, so the game becomes a two-sided
  hazard war rather than a one-sided hazard lock.

Positioning and Explosion trade, turns 17-24:

- underlying tries to use Zapdos with Reflect and Hidden Power against
  Tyranitar/Steelix positioning.
- Steelix Roars Zapdos out on turn 22, then Explodes into the Zapdos switch on
  turn 24.
- This removes a major pressure piece, but Steelix is also gone, and underlying
  still has Gengar plus Tyranitar as possible converters.

Luck-heavy midgame conversion, turns 25-30:

- RUBYBLOOD's Zapdos misses Thunder into Snorlax on turn 25, then crit-KOs
  Snorlax on turn 26.
- underlying's poisoned Tyranitar answers by beating Zapdos with Rock Slide
  through several Thunder misses, then Surfing opposing Tyranitar.
- Do not grade this phase by outcome alone. The useful lesson is that the
  original Spikes layer and Toxic timer left Tyranitar able to convert even
  after Zapdos and Snorlax were gone.

RestTalk anchor fight, turns 31-46:

- RUBYBLOOD's poisoned Snorlax Rests on turn 31 and enters the RestTalk loop.
- underlying brings Gengar into Sleep Talk Double-Edge on turn 32, exploiting
  the Normal immunity.
- Sleep Talk repeatedly calls Rest and resets Snorlax's health/sleep position,
  so Gengar cannot treat sleep as ownership. It has to keep re-pricing the
  route through Thunderbolt, Dynamic Punch, confusion, wake turns, and Rest.
- On turn 46, Gengar Explodes to remove Snorlax. The Explosion is a route
  handoff: Gengar stops being the hazard/spinblock piece and spends itself so
  Tyranitar can finish.

Endgame, turns 47-53:

- Forretress gets one Rapid Spin on turn 47 before dying, removing underlying's
  Spikes.
- The spin matters, but it is too late to fully erase the damage and status
  already created. Tyranitar still has enough immediate pressure to beat
  Alakazam through Recover from turns 49-53.

## Critical Turns

Turn 5:

- Cloyster sets Spikes instead of attacking or preserving itself. The critical
  Double-Edge makes the outcome brutal, but the pre-turn logic is defensible:
  Snorlax was already poisoned, and the layer would tax Tyranitar, Forretress,
  Alakazam, Steelix, and Snorlax for the rest of the game unless removed.

Turn 10:

- Gengar blocks Rapid Spin. This is the first clean route-preserving
  positioning turn. The value is not "Gengar walls Forretress"; the value is
  "Spikes stay long enough to turn later grounded entries into thresholds."

Turn 24:

- Steelix Explodes into Zapdos. This is a strong conversion for RUBYBLOOD
  because Zapdos was a major remaining pressure piece. The cost is losing the
  Steelix phaze/Ground route before Tyranitar and Gengar are solved.

Turns 31-46:

- Gengar versus RestTalk Snorlax is a long re-score drill. Sleep Talk Rest calls
  mean direct damage can be erased, while wake turns mean Gengar cannot rely on
  a static script. Explosion becomes correct only when the remaining route map
  says Tyranitar can inherit the endgame.

Turn 47:

- Forretress spins before dying. This is not proof the earlier spinblock failed.
  Partial retention was enough: Spikes had already forced multiple entries,
  contributed to Snorlax and Tyranitar thresholds, and created the conditions
  for Tyranitar's endgame.

## Practical Lessons

- A hazard route can be partially successful. You do not always need Spikes to
  last until the final turn; you need them to last until they change the
  threshold or clock that your next route uses.
- Spinblocking is a timed job. Gengar did not need to deny every Rapid Spin
  forever. It needed to deny the early spin, force damage, and then hand the
  game to Tyranitar after Snorlax was removed.
- RestTalk anchors require repeated re-scoring. Sleep Talk calling Rest can
  erase damage and reset the fight, so the attacking side needs either PP,
  status, trapping, Explosion, or a second route ready.
- Explosion is strongest as a handoff. Gengar's turn-46 Explosion works because
  the answer to "who wins after Gengar is gone?" is poisoned Tyranitar into
  Forretress/Alakazam.
- Do not review luck-heavy phases only by result. Zapdos Thunder misses and
  crits matter, but the strategic review is whether each side had a coherent
  next route after the luck branch.

## Cookbook Update

Updated `Recipe: Two-Sided Hazard War` with a partial-retention rule:

```text
Blocking the first spin can be enough if the hazard has already changed a KO
range, Rest cycle, switch tax, or endgame route. If the spinner survives to spin
later, immediately hand off to the route that the earlier hazard turns enabled.
```
