## Phase Review

Early damage opens the hazard game, turns 1-27:

- Zapdos pressures Snorlax early and forces Rest on turn 4.
- Cloyster sets Spikes on turn 6.
- Forretress removes the first layer on turn 14, then Raikou and Snorlax trade
  Rest / Sleep Talk turns into Zapdos and Snorlax.
- This phase mostly builds the public map: both sides have RestTalk anchors,
  both sides have hazard control, and neither side has revealed the decisive
  Espeon pass route yet.

The spin war decides entry freedom, turns 28-38:

- Forretress sets Spikes on underlying's side on turn 28.
- Cloyster sets Spikes back, but Forretress removes them on turns 30 and 31.
- Starmie also spins on turn 33 when Zapdos enters.
- Golem later spins away melancholy0's layer on turn 38.
- The important lesson is that spin turns are not just bookkeeping. They decide
  which side can afford the repeated pivots that a Baton Pass / phaze fight
  will demand.

Skarmory absorbs the midgame, turns 40-61:

- Exeggutor paralyzes Skarmory on turn 40, but Skarmory still pressures with
  Drill Peck.
- Snorlax and Skarmory trade low-impact but route-relevant damage and recovery
  turns.
- Cloyster Explodes into sleeping Skarmory on turn 61, taking Skarmory from
  full to 55% rather than removing it.
- Underlying spends a one-time route trade, but melancholy0 preserves the key
  anti-pass structure: Tyranitar is still available for Roar, and Skarmory is
  still alive as a bulky pivot.

Espeon starts the pass route, turns 62-79:

- Espeon uses Growth on turns 62 and 63.
- Tyranitar Roars on turn 63, dragging Golem and breaking the immediate pass
  route.
- Espeon returns and repeats the pattern: Growth on turns 72-73, then Roar
  drags Zapdos on turn 73.
- On turn 76, Espeon Baton Passes to Zapdos, but Tyranitar Roars the receiver
  and drags Espeon back.
- On turn 78, Espeon Baton Passes to Golem, and Roar drags Zapdos.
- The phazing is good, but not self-proving. It buys time only because
  Tyranitar stays healthy and because the receiver map can still be attacked
  or reset afterward.

The pass route keeps restarting, turns 80-99:

- Golem, Snorlax, Zapdos, and Espeon keep cycling as potential receivers or
  pivots.
- Tyranitar alternates Roar, Curse, and Rock Slide. It does not reduce the
  whole problem to "click Roar"; it keeps enough HP and offensive pressure to
  matter after a pass attempt.
- Skarmory RestTalk gives melancholy0 another stabilizer while Tyranitar
  remains the anti-pass endpoint.
- Espeon passes to Zapdos again on turns 91 and 99. Tyranitar Roars both
  receivers before the boosts convert.

Tyranitar turns control into an endpoint, turns 100-109:

- Tyranitar Curses on turn 100 as Espeon Growths again.
- Underlying uses Golem to pressure, but Tyranitar and Skarmory absorb the
  sequence.
- On turn 108, Espeon Baton Passes to sleeping Zapdos, and Tyranitar chooses
  Rock Slide instead of another Roar. Zapdos drops from 80% to 17%.
- Zapdos wakes on turn 109 and uses Thunder, but Tyranitar survives at 22% and
  KOs with Rock Slide.
- This proves the endpoint: repeated phazing was the bridge. The win came when
  the phazer had enough HP, boosts, and damage to remove the receiver.

## Critical Turns

Turns 30-33:

- Forretress and Starmie remove Spikes multiple times. Without those spin
  turns, the later anti-pass pivoting would have cost too much HP.

Turn 61:

- Cloyster Explodes into Skarmory, but the trade does not remove the anti-pass
  route. It spends a one-time resource without breaking Tyranitar's Roar path.

Turns 63 and 73:

- Tyranitar Roars after Espeon uses Growth. These turns stop the chain before a
  receiver can act with the boosts.

Turns 76, 78, 91, and 99:

- Baton Pass reaches a receiver, but Tyranitar Roars the receiver before it can
  convert.

Turn 108:

- Tyranitar attacks the receiver instead of reflexively phazing. This is the
  payoff turn: once Zapdos is in range, damage becomes better than another
  reset.

## Practical Lessons

Anti-pass control needs an endpoint:

- Roar repeatedly stopped Espeon, but the route was not "Roar forever." The
  endpoint was preserving Tyranitar long enough to attack the receiver and
  survive the return hit.

Receiver map beats passer tunnel vision:

- Espeon was the support source, but the dangerous boards were Zapdos or Golem
  receiving boosts. Good advice has to name both the passer and the receivers.

Hazard control funds the answer:

- The early spins gave Tyranitar, Skarmory, Starmie, and Forretress enough
  entries to keep answering the chain. If the entry tax had stayed up, the same
  phazing plan might have collapsed.

Trade value depends on what it breaks:

- Cloyster Explosion damaged Skarmory but did not remove the Roar endpoint. A
  one-time trade is not decisive unless it deletes the route piece that matters
  or delivers the receiver before that piece can act.

Switch from reset to damage when the receiver is in range:

- Turn 108 is the key live-turn habit. Once the receiver is exposed and damage
  creates the win, continuing to phaze can be worse than attacking.

## Boss Transfer

- Bugsy's Ledian and Sabrina's Espeon both create local Baton Pass questions.
  The transferable rule is not "always phaze pass." It is "name the receiver,
  prove the phaze/Haze/Encore/attack can resolve, and state the endpoint."
- Local Baton Pass source confirms the move needs another living party member,
  switches, applies entry hazards to the incoming enemy, and resets some
  non-passed temporary states such as Disable, attraction, Encore, Transform,
  and wrap counts. Exact Gym Leader Lab advice must use that source, not only
  Smogon memory.
- Local Quiver Dance is +1 SpA / +1 SpD / +1 Spe, so Bugsy's Ledian support
  can change both Speed and bulk before Scyther receives it.
- Boss AI must not use hidden player-team knowledge to decide whether a Baton
  Pass chain has a receiver or whether the player has a hidden phazer/Haze
  answer. It can use public HP, status, hazards, revealed moves, observed
  switches/faints, and source-allowed memory.

## New Notebook Lesson

Phazing a support chain is a bridge, not an endpoint. To beat Baton Pass-style
support, name the receiver map, preserve the control user, and know when to
stop resetting and start damaging the exposed receiver.
