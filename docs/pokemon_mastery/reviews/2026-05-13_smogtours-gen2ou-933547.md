# Review: smogtours-gen2ou-933547

Source: https://replay.pokemonshowdown.com/smogtours-gen2ou-933547

Forum source:
https://www.smogon.com/forums/threads/gsc-ou-winter-seasonal-8-round-13-losers-bracket-replays-required.3781520/

Format: vanilla GSC OU.

Players:

- tylerkehne
- RUBYBLOOD

Result: tylerkehne won on turn 239.

## Team Shapes

tylerkehne revealed:

- Misdreavus: Mean Look / Perish Song / Destiny Bond / Rest
- Forretress: Spikes / Rapid Spin / Hidden Power
- Blastoise: Rapid Spin / Rest
- Skarmory: Sand Attack / Curse / Drill Peck
- Jolteon: Growth / Thunderbolt / Rest / Sleep Talk
- Snorlax: Double-Edge / Surf / Rest / Sleep Talk

RUBYBLOOD revealed:

- Forretress: Protect / Spikes / Toxic / Hidden Power
- Tentacruel: Rapid Spin / Toxic / Surf / Protect
- Raikou: Thunderbolt / Hidden Power / Rest / Sleep Talk
- Skarmory: Toxic / Sand Attack / Rest / Sleep Talk
- Snorlax: Curse / Return / Rest / Sleep Talk
- Tyranitar: Earthquake / Pursuit

## Phase Map

Opening trap, turns 1-8:

- tylerkehne uses Misdreavus to trap RUBYBLOOD's Forretress with Mean Look and
  Perish Song.
- Misdreavus is poisoned and forced to Rest, but Forretress faints on turn 8.
- The trade removes one hazard setter early, but RUBYBLOOD still has
  Tentacruel as a spinner, so this is not a solved hazard game.

Hazard removal reset, turns 9-24:

- Tentacruel immediately spins away tylerkehne's Spikes on turn 9.
- Blastoise removes RUBYBLOOD's Spikes on turn 10, then Rests after Toxic.
- The game shifts from "who set Spikes first?" to "who can keep creating
  hazard turns without losing the converter or spinner map?"

Jolteon preservation loop, turns 18-46:

- Jolteon is paralyzed by Raikou on turn 18 and immediately Rests on turn 19,
  converting a route-killing status into a temporary sleep clock.
- From there, tylerkehne repeatedly rotates Jolteon, Skarmory, Forretress, and
  Snorlax through Raikou / Snorlax / Skarmory answers instead of spending
  Jolteon for one short attack.
- Jolteon wakes and finds Growth attempts, but Snorlax repeatedly forces it
  back out.

Long hazard/spin contest, turns 70-159:

- Forretress repeatedly sets Spikes; Tentacruel repeatedly removes them.
- tylerkehne sometimes uses Misdreavus as a spinblocker, but it is asleep and
  cannot freely convert the block into a permanent hazard state.
- This section is not wasted play. It keeps Tentacruel and the opposing answer
  map busy while tylerkehne preserves Jolteon and Snorlax as late routes.

Anchor exhaustion, turns 123-180:

- RUBYBLOOD's Raikou, Snorlax, and Skarmory are all pushed into Rest cycles.
- tylerkehne's Snorlax and Skarmory keep checking those anchors without
  overcommitting Jolteon.
- On turn 179, Skarmory poisons Jolteon, but Jolteon Rests immediately on turn
  180. The status lands, but it does not break the route because Rest was still
  available and the team had enough support to cover the sleep turns.

Conversion, turns 194-239:

- Jolteon pressures Tyranitar with Sleep Talk Thunderbolt and later direct
  Thunderbolt, then catches Snorlax with paralysis on turn 200.
- The paralysis outcome is lucky, but the route exists because Jolteon was
  preserved for over 180 turns instead of being spent earlier.
- After another long spin/recovery loop, Jolteon wakes on turn 236, uses Growth
  twice into sleeping Snorlax, and wins with Thunderbolt on turns 238-239.

## Critical Turns

Turn 4:

- RUBYBLOOD uses Protect as Misdreavus uses Mean Look. From public information,
  Misdreavus trap / Perish Song is a plausible route, so this is the first
  material route concession: Forretress becomes trapped without making damage,
  status, or switch progress that turn.
- I am not labeling this a decisive blunder by itself. RUBYBLOOD still had
  Tentacruel to spin, and Forretress had already put up Spikes. The lesson is
  narrower: a passive turn into a known trapping species must be priced as
  losing the current support piece.

Turns 9-10:

- Both sides remove Spikes. This is the first major re-score point after the
  successful Perish trap.
- The correct thought is not "we killed the Spiker, so hazards are won." It is
  "their remaining spinner now defines whether the trap converted."

Turns 18-19:

- Jolteon gets paralyzed by Raikou but immediately uses Rest. This is strong
  route preservation: the player does not treat paralysis as merely bad luck,
  and does not throw Jolteon into a doomed exchange.
- Rest turns become the cost of keeping Jolteon as a long-game converter.

Turns 70-112:

- Forretress and Tentacruel repeat the Spikes / Rapid Spin contest. The surface
  result looks like no progress, but the route map is changing: PP, HP,
  revealed moves, spinblock timing, and the health of Jolteon / Snorlax all
  matter.
- This is a good example of long-game Pokemon not being one-turn scoreboard
  play. Repeated "no progress" turns can still preserve the only route that
  matters later.

Turns 123-136:

- RUBYBLOOD's Raikou and Snorlax enter Rest cycles. Snorlax even Sleep Talks
  Rest multiple times.
- This reinforces the GSC RestTalk lesson: damaging a sleeping RestTalk anchor
  is only progress if the damage, PP, status, hazards, or setup opportunity
  survives the Sleep Talk -> Rest reset branch.

Turns 179-180:

- Jolteon is poisoned by Skarmory and immediately Rests. This is the second
  time status could have killed the Jolteon route but did not, because the
  recovery plan and support structure were still intact.

Turns 194-200:

- Jolteon stays active long enough to pressure Tyranitar and then paralyze
  Snorlax with Thunderbolt.
- The paralysis itself is outcome luck, but the decision structure is not
  random: tylerkehne preserved the converter until the opposing Electric check,
  Pursuit piece, and Snorlax route were all stretched.

Turns 236-239:

- Jolteon wakes, uses Growth twice, and finally converts through sleeping
  Snorlax.
- This is the payoff for the whole battle's preservation policy. The final
  route was visible much earlier, but it was not safe until the answer map had
  been worn down and Snorlax was forced into the right sleep state.

## Practical Lessons

Preserve the converter through ugly turns:

- A win condition can spend most of the battle sleeping, switching, or being
  denied. That does not make it dead. It is dead only when its required HP,
  PP, status, matchup, or support structure is gone.

A successful early trap still needs a conversion check:

- Removing Forretress was valuable, but Tentacruel's Rapid Spin meant hazards
  were not automatically secured. After any trap or trade, ask what remaining
  piece still performs the lost role.

Status can be a route reset, not only a route killer:

- Jolteon absorbed paralysis and poison at different points, but Rest converted
  both into sleep clocks. The real question was whether the team could cover
  those sleep turns.

Loops need a purpose:

- The long Spikes / Rapid Spin / Rest loop was not inherently good. It was good
  only because it preserved tylerkehne's endgame converter while exhausting and
  revealing the opposing anchors.

## Romhack Transfer

- Boss fights often have fewer perfect competitive pivots, but the route lesson
  transfers directly: do not spend the future converter just because the current
  turn looks inactive.
- Against Gym Leader Lab bosses, a sleeping or temporarily statused player
  Pokemon may still be the correct long-term win route if Rest, Sleep Talk,
  cleric support, item support, or safe pivoting can cover the inactive turns.
- Conversely, killing a boss support Pokemon does not prove the plan worked if
  another boss Pokemon still performs the same route job. For example, removing
  one hazard setter, rain setter, screen setter, sleep user, or recovery anchor
  must be followed by a check for the remaining roster.

## New Notebook Lesson

Do not confuse "currently denied" with "no longer a win condition." A converter
that is asleep, statused, forced out, or blocked by an answer can still be the
main route if the rest of the game is being played to preserve its eventual
entry and remove its remaining blockers.
