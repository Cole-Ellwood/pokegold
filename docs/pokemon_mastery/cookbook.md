## Information Model Guardrail

Gym Leader Lab and vanilla GSC do not have symmetrical Team Preview.

Use different information models depending on who is being advised:

- Player-side boss advice may use the boss roster, fixed/adaptive opening
  policy, and local source docs before turn 1 because those are trainer data
  the advisor can inspect.
- Boss AI advice must not use the unrevealed player team, moves, items, or box.
  It can only use public battle state, revealed player species/moves, observed
  damage/status/switches, and source-legal priors where the AI is explicitly
  allowed to infer.
- Vanilla GSC replay review starts from the actual lead and revealed teams over
  time, not from later-generation Team Preview.

Later-generation Team Preview articles are analogy material only. Transfer the
habit of route triage from known information; do not transfer the mechanic
that both sides see all six Pokemon at battle start.

## Recipe: Turn-1 Game Plan

Trigger:

- A battle starts, or a planned boss fight has source-known roster/opening
  policy before turn 1.

Steps:

1. Name our plausible winning routes, not just our strongest Pokemon.
2. Name the opponent routes that can beat us if left alone.
3. Identify the blockers to each route: defensive answers, phazers, spinners,
   sleep absorbers, recovery loops, PP, or damage thresholds.
4. Decide which early resource matters most: hazards, status, information,
   preserving a wall, forcing Rest, removing a blocker, or denying setup.
5. For a planned boss fight, list the boss's fixed opener or local
   source-supported adaptive opener family. Filter legal openers by the boss
   opening policy and incentives; do not assume the boss sees the player's
   unrevealed team.
6. Choose a lead and first move that improve the route while keeping the
   opponent's best route covered.
7. Write the abandon conditions: revealed coverage, unexpected damage, miss,
   early wake, crit, switch pattern, or a newly visible route.

Failure signs:

- The plan is "do damage" with no named endgame.
- The plan assumes one check answers every set.
- Taking a KO gives the opponent a free setup turn that our plan cannot handle.

Artifact:

- Use `pre_battle_route_sheet.md` before a planned boss fight. It converts this
  recipe into a compact input/output sheet.

Quality gate:

- A turn-1 plan is ready only if it names the first route, the roadblock to that
  route, the opening job, the first expected boss response, the piece that must
  not be spent, and the condition that would force a re-plan. Smogon's Team
  Preview and lead material translate only as route-triage habits: use
  source-known boss rosters, not a nonexistent Gen 2 preview mechanic or hidden
  player-team knowledge. If the plan cannot answer "then what?", it is still
  only a matchup note.

## Recipe: Boss Route Triage

Trigger:

- The advisor knows the boss roster or source-supported opener family before a
  planned boss fight.

Rule:

- Do not choose the lead by the first opposing Pokemon alone. Sort the boss
  routes by urgency, cost of denial, and which player piece each route demands.
  Then choose the opening that denies the route that becomes hardest to stop
  after one free turn.

Source basis:

- Team Preview material contributes only an analogy: when roster information is
  legitimately known, infer route machinery, identify roadblocks, estimate
  opener incentives, and keep the assessment flexible. It does not mean Gen 2
  or Gym Leader Lab has team preview, and it does not let boss AI know the
  unrevealed player team.
- GSC team-structure material treats teams as recurring role packages: spiker,
  electric, boom/utility, normal resist, offensive sweeper, defensive glue, and
  cleric support. The transferable idea is to read a roster as route machinery,
  not as six isolated species.
- Synergy/core material separates type synergy from check/counter synergy. For
  boss fights, this means a piece may be valuable because it answers a route,
  even if it is not the best type matchup into the lead.
- Win-condition planning says the opening should make the eventual route more
  realistic, not merely win the visible one-on-one.

Classify each boss route:

```text
immediate:
  Can win or cripple the plan right now if not denied.

accumulating:
  Becomes dangerous after hazards, poison, screens, weather, recovery, PP, or
  repeated forced switches.

endgame:
  Needs support or chip first, but must be preserved against from turn 1.
```

Ask:

1. Which route becomes unanswerable after one free turn?
2. Which route can be delayed safely because the answer is durable or redundant?
3. Which of our pieces answers multiple routes, and which answers only one?
4. Which route does the boss lead help enable if we only attack it?
5. Which player resource is cheapest to spend now: HP, status, PP, item,
   hazard turn, information, or a sack?
6. What route becomes live if our lead is poisoned, paralyzed, slept, trapped,
   locked, or chipped?
7. What information would reorder the route priority?

Decision rule:

- Deny the immediate route first when it creates irreversible loss.
- Deny the accumulating route first when one free support turn makes several
  later answers collapse.
- Preserve for the endgame route when the current exchange tempts you to spend
  the only answer before the endgame piece appears.
- If two routes demand the same answer, avoid leading that answer unless it
  prevents a worse route immediately.

Shared-answer budget:

- When multiple boss routes point at one answer, count that answer's remaining
  entries and actions before using it. Hazards, poison, paralysis, weather,
  recovery PP, forced locks, and chip can turn "we have an answer" into "we had
  one answer twice." Create a backup, shorten the game, or preserve the shared
  answer unless the current route is more urgent or the spend forces the win.

Failure signs:

- The plan says "beat the lead" without naming which later route this exposes.
- A route is ranked by species fear instead of board urgency.
- The same player Pokemon is assigned to stop two incompatible boss routes.
- The first move wins a one-on-one but gives a setup, recovery, weather,
  hazard, or priority route the exact board it wanted.

## Recipe: Known-Set Threatlist Audit

Trigger:

- A boss roster, trainer set list, or simulator validation plan is available
  before battle.

Rule:

- Before trusting a turn-1 plan, audit every high-severity boss set that can
  beat the team if the primary route is disrupted. Each threat needs a
  reasonable play line: the answer, the entry path, the backup if that answer
  is crippled, and the information that would flip the line.

Source lesson:

- Smogon's Battle Maison singles advice is directly useful for boss fights:
  small-team in-game formats punish glaring weaknesses, known trainer/set data
  should be checked before play, and when multiple sets are possible the safer
  line is often the one punished least by guessing wrong.
- Maison variance guidance also transfers: if the matchup is already favorable,
  choose the line that minimizes the bad branch. Do not rely on repeated
  inaccurate moves, status coinflips, or greedy setup unless safe lines lose.
- Smogon's threatlist guide warns that type synergy alone is incomplete. A
  boss threat can be the coverage move that hits the supposed answer, a bulky
  set the team cannot break, a two-Pokemon core that removes each other's
  checks, or a hazard plan that makes repeated entries impossible.

Ask:

1. Which boss Pokemon or set beats us if plan A misses, gets crit, gets
   statused, or meets the wrong hidden move?
2. What exact piece answers it, and can that piece enter after hazards,
   weather, status, and expected chip?
3. If one boss Pokemon has multiple plausible sets, which move is punished
   least if our set read is wrong?
4. Does every dangerous boss route have a reasonable play line, or are we
   accepting a forced-risk matchup?
5. Which unknown ability, item, passive, speed, or move would change the lead
   or first move?
6. Is the proposed high-damage line worse than a lower-variance two-turn line
   because a miss, status, or critical hit loses the whole fight?
7. Is the threat defensive, offensive, utility, core-based, or hazard-based?
8. Can we actually break the boss anchor, or only avoid dying to it?

Decision rule:

- Mark the route plan ready only when each high-severity threat is either
  covered by a durable answer, denied before it acts, converted into a known
  sack/route trade, or labeled as forced risk with the best available
  mitigation.
- When set uncertainty changes the correct move, choose the line with the best
  worst-plausible branch unless the matchup is already losing without risk.
- Treat simulator win rate as untrusted if the preregistered team has an
  unaudited glaring weakness to a known boss threat.
- Do not clear a threat because the type chart looks favorable. Clear it only
  after checking coverage, recovery, status, item/passive behavior, hazards,
  speed, and whether the user's team has enough damage or control to convert.

Failure signs:

- The plan says "we probably sweep" but cannot name the answer to one backline
  boss Pokemon.
- A known boss set is ignored because the lead matchup looks favorable.
- The threatlist says "covered by type" while the boss set has coverage,
  recovery, status, or hazard support that breaks that answer.
- The line needs several inaccurate moves or repeated status turns even though
  a stable lower-variance route exists.
- The advisor blames a loss on bad luck without checking whether the
  threatlist already exposed the bad branch.

## Recipe: Lead Role Fit Test

Trigger:

- Choosing a lead for a planned boss fight or evaluating whether the current
  lead is doing the correct job.

Rule:

- A lead is not the Pokemon that best wins turn 1. A lead is the Pokemon whose
  first-turn job fits the whole plan while preserving the piece needed for the
  boss's later route.

Source lesson:

- Smogon's lead articles repeatedly frame lead choice as team-dependent: the
  lead sets the pace, but it is chosen after the team's structure and needs are
  known. Reliability, versatility, and later-game usability are tradeoffs, not
  cosmetic details.
- Smogon's GSC lead analysis is a no-preview lesson. Common leads are chosen
  because they create useful first contact across a likely opener field:
  paralysis, sleep, Thief, Spikes, phazing/scouting, forcing a switch, or
  early route pressure. The goal is not to pick a perfect anti-lead from a
  fully revealed board.

Ask:

1. What exact job must the lead do: deny setup, set hazards, absorb status,
   force damage, scout, pivot, or preserve another piece?
2. If the lead wins the visible one-on-one, what boss route becomes easier or
   harder afterward?
3. Is the lead also the only answer to the boss's ace, weather payoff, setup
   sweeper, sleep route, or priority cleaner?
4. If the lead loses the opening matchup, is there a planned recovery route, or
   does the fight start with a panic switch?
5. Does the lead still have a useful later role after turn 1?
6. Is the lead being chosen for speed or damage alone, while a slower lead
   would preserve the real win route better?
7. If the opener is not fully known, what contact value does the lead still
   create in the bad matchup: information, status, hazards, item removal,
   phazing, forced switch, or safe pivot tempo?

Decision rule:

- Lead with the cleaner only if the cleaner's route is already open or the
  opening threat must be removed immediately.
- Lead with the defensive answer only if it cannot be crippled before its later
  job matters, or if no other piece can stop the opening route.
- Lead with a support Pokemon only if its support changes the boss route before
  the boss gets a stronger setup, status, recovery, or weather turn.
- Lead with a scout or pivot when the most valuable turn-1 result is
  information plus preservation, not chip damage.

Failure signs:

- The lead is selected because it is fastest or strongest, but it is also the
  only later answer.
- The first turn creates generic damage while the boss gets the route that
  actually wins.
- The plan requires switching out immediately with no safe pivot and no gain in
  information.
- The lead performs a narrow anti-lead job and then becomes dead weight before
  its trade has advanced a concrete route.

Boss application:

- Against Bugsy, a lead that beats Ariados but gets poisoned or weakened as the
  only Scyther answer is doing the wrong job.
- Against Misty, a lead that pressures Politoed but is the only Starmie/Lapras
  answer may hand the fight to Hypnosis or rain.
- Against Red, a lead that beats Pikachu but loses the Snorlax, Espeon, sun, or
  Blastoise plan is not actually winning the opening.

## Recipe: Opening Move As Resource Bid

Trigger:

- Turn 1, a new lead matchup, or a forced fresh entry after a KO.

Rule:

- Treat the opening move as a bid for one resource: damage threshold,
  information, hazard position, status, safer entry, setup denial, or
  preservation. The best opening is not always the move that wins the visible
  one-on-one.

Decision test:

- If a move forces the boss to reveal a pivot, healing line, status plan, or
  damage threshold while preserving our later answer, it can be better than
  immediate chip.
- If the move exposes the only endgame piece before its blockers are known or
  weakened, the lead is doing the wrong job even if it looks strong on turn 1.
- If the opening plan depends on a romhack type or passive claim, verify the
  local mechanic before calling the matchup favorable.

Failure signs:

- Leading the cleaner because it is fast, then losing its HP/status before the
  route is open.
- Setting a hazard or status because it is generally useful while the boss gets
  an immediate setup, recovery, or lock-in route.
- Calling a turn-1 double or prediction good without naming what information or
  board state makes the risk worth taking.

## Recipe: Probe Then Convert

Trigger:

- A move does little or no immediate damage but could reduce a dangerous branch:
  accuracy drop, scouting move, Protect, pivot, screen, status probe, or other
  information/safety turn.

Rule:

- Probe only when the probe changes the branch that decides the route, and the
  next turn has a concrete conversion plan. A probe is not progress by itself.

Source basis:

- Smogon's risk/reward and prediction articles frame strong play as comparing
  likely actions, worst-case branches, and reward for being right. Smogon's lead
  material adds the opening tradeoff: a turn-1 choice must fit the full battle
  plan, not only the visible matchup.

Check:

1. What exact branch does the probe reduce or reveal?
2. How severe is that branch if we do not probe?
3. What does the probe cost if the opponent attacks, sets up, switches, heals,
   or ignores it?
4. Does the probe survive bad luck: miss, crit, low roll, early wake, or AI
   choosing the worst public move?
5. If the probe works, what is the next move that converts it into damage,
   denial, safe entry, recovery, or route progress?
6. Can the opponent clear or invalidate the probe for free?

Decision rule:

- Probe when direct damage barely changes the route, the opponent has a
  plausible swing branch, the probe immediately lowers that branch, and the next
  turn can convert.
- Attack, switch, or recover instead when the opponent is already in a decisive
  threshold, the probe can be cleared cheaply, or the probe gives the boss a
  stronger setup, sleep, recovery, weather, hazard, or switch route.
- After one successful probe, re-score. Do not repeat the probe unless the next
  probe changes a new threshold.
- If the probe changes accuracy, re-score hit reliability and ask whether
  switching, poison, phazing, Protect, PP, or direct damage is now the clearer
  endpoint.
- If the opponent stays in despite a weak revealed move, treat that stay as
  information about possible hidden coverage. Preserve first when the hidden
  branch is plausible, severe, and direct damage does not force the route.

Failure signs:

- The explanation says "be safe" but cannot name the branch being reduced.
- The probe works, but there is no next-turn conversion plan.
- The same debuff or scout move is repeated while the opponent can switch,
  recover, set up, or clear the value.
- An accuracy drop is treated as the win condition without naming the miss,
  receiver, clock, PP route, or KO threshold it buys.
- The probe user is an irreplaceable piece and the wrong branch spends its
  required HP/status/PP.

## Recipe: Non-Action Evidence Test

Trigger:

- The opponent stays in despite a weak revealed move, skips an expected switch,
  declines recovery/status/setup, repeats a low-impact line, or withholds a
  move that would obviously help if it were safe.

Rule:

- Treat non-actions as evidence, not proof. Ask what visible incentive, boss AI
  scoring reason, forced state, PP/status constraint, hidden move, hidden item,
  or future route makes the non-action rational. Then price the hidden branch
  by legality, plausibility, severity, and incentive.

Source basis:

- Smogon's prediction and risk/reward articles frame prediction as information
  plus risk/reward, not blind guessing.
- Smogon's GSC planning material stresses that the current click should fit a
  long-term route instead of only the next visible damage trade.
- The Clair Dragonair/Suicune worked example shows a local boss-state version:
  if the player's revealed Surf is weak but Suicune stays in, hidden Ice
  coverage becomes a branch Clair should respect without treating it as
  confirmed.

Ask:

1. What action did I expect the opponent to take, and why?
2. What did the opponent actually not do?
3. Is the non-action explained by visible incentives, lock-in, PP, status,
   hazards, speed, AI scoring, or current route needs?
4. If not, what hidden move, item, passive, set, or damage threshold becomes
   more plausible?
5. If that branch is real, does it remove a route, an irreplaceable piece, or
   an endgame clock?
6. What move preserves the route while testing or re-scoring the belief?
7. What new information would downgrade, confirm, or dismiss the branch?

Decision rule:

- Preserve or pivot when the hidden branch is legal, plausible, severe,
  incentivized, and direct damage does not force a decisive route.
- Attack or convert when the hidden branch is low-plausibility, already covered
  by another piece, or respecting it gives up the only live route.
- Probe when the cost of testing the branch is lower than the cost of being
  wrong and there is a concrete next-turn conversion plan.
- Never write the hidden branch as fact until it is revealed, mechanically
  implied, or forced by damage/speed evidence.

Failure signs:

- "They did not reveal it, so they do not have it."
- "They stayed in, so they definitely have it."
- Treating a boss AI non-action as a human bluff without local AI evidence.
- Ignoring the hidden branch because the visible move does more immediate
  damage.
- Respecting every legal hidden move even when the cost of doing so loses the
  current route.

## Recipe: Boss AI Opponent Model

Trigger:

- Advising a Gym Leader Lab boss turn where local AI traces, boss roster data,
  or AI tier behavior may change the opponent model.

Rule:

- Do not treat the boss as a random cartridge trainer, a perfect competitive
  human, or a fixed script. Build a three-part opponent model: source-known
  roster route, current public-state incentive, and any validated trace
  evidence for this exact boss/state class.

Source basis:

- Smogon's prediction and risk/reward articles treat strong prediction as
  weighing likely actions, worst-case actions, and long-term consequences, not
  guessing blindly.
- Smogon's introductory competitive guide stresses that strong play requires
  mechanics knowledge and consideration of both short- and long-term threats.
- Local Boss AI design promises public-info reasoning: visible species, HP,
  status, field state, revealed moves, public type chart, learnability priors,

Separate these claims:

```text
Roster route:
  What the boss team is built to make happen.

Likely AI move:
  What current trace/source/scoring evidence suggests the AI may choose.

Worst route move:
  The legal move that most damages our plan if chosen.

Trace evidence:
  Exact artifact and whether it is first-decision, scenario-specific, or live.

  The move that covers the route, not merely the predicted click.
```

Decision rule:

- If the trace suggests a likely move but another public move would be

- If the trace ledger audit fails, treat existing trace files as historical

- Saying "the AI will click X" without trace/source evidence.

## Recipe: Win-Condition Map

Trigger:

- Any serious turn where a move looks obviously good.

Rule:

- Treat a win condition as a route through remaining blockers, not as a species
  slogan.
- A win condition is the board state that lets a route win. The Pokemon or move
  is only the converter.

Source basis:

- Smogon's win-condition discussion says to identify the opponent threats at
  the beginning, decide how to beat them, then identify what can threaten after
  those threats are handled.
- Smogon's prediction/planning article says a game plan should exist before
  the first move, but it must have backups because the opponent can disrupt the
  original plan.
- Smogon's risk/reward article adds the posture check: when the matchup is
  favorable, reduce avoidable risk until the route is secure; when the matchup
  is poor, accepting risk may be necessary because passive safety loses.

Use this shape:

```text
Our route:
  primary piece:
  blockers:
  required resources:
  next step:
  what kills the route:

Opponent route:
  primary piece:
  blockers:
  required resources:
  next step:
  what denies the route:
```

Decision test:

- A move is strong when it removes a blocker, preserves a required resource,
  forces the opponent into a worse route, or improves an endgame clock.
- A move is suspect when it wins the current turn but weakens the only answer to
  the opponent's best remaining route.
- A safe-looking move is still wrong if it never creates the board state the
  route requires.
- A risky move can be correct only when it either forces the route state or
  prevents an opponent route that safe play cannot cover.
- A converter does not have to win on its first entry. If the first entry
  removes a blocker, forces Rest, creates a hazard or status threshold, or
  exposes the opponent's counter-route while the support ladder remains intact,
  the route may still be live.

Ask:

1. What state must exist for our route to win?
2. Which opposing piece, status, hazard, PP count, speed relation, or recovery
   loop still blocks that state?
3. Which opponent route wins if we spend this turn on our own plan?
4. Are we ahead enough to minimize risk, or behind enough that safe play just
   loses slowly?
5. What move this turn changes the blocker map rather than only the HP total?

## Recipe: Denied Is Not Dead

Trigger:

- A planned converter is asleep, statused, forced out, walled, phazed, spin
  denied, or temporarily checked by the current board.

Rule:

- Classify the route as live, paused, damaged, or dead before handing off. A
  route is paused when its converter still has the HP, PP, status condition,
  recovery, support, and blocker-removal path it needs. A route is dead only
  when a required resource is gone or the remaining blockers cannot be removed
  without taking a worse risk.

Check:

1. What exact piece converts this route?
2. What is denying it right now: HP, status, sleep turns, PP, hazards, a wall,
   a phazer, a spinner, speed, or hidden-info risk?
3. Can that blocker still be worn down, statused, trapped, forced to Rest,
   PP-stalled, lured, or removed by a trade?
4. Can the rest of the team cover the converter while it sleeps, heals,
   pivots, or waits for the blocker to weaken?
5. What observation would prove the route is dead and make backup handoff
   mandatory?

Decision rule:

- Preserve or recover the converter when the route is paused and the support
  path still exists.
- Handoff when the route is dead, or when the backup route now has fewer
  blockers and lower catastrophe risk.
- Spend a turn on hazards, recovery, pivoting, or PP only when it preserves the
  route or weakens the thing currently denying it.

Failure signs:

- Declaring a route dead because the converter is asleep, low, or forced out
  once.
- Repeating setup or attacks into the same blocker without changing the blocker
  map.
- Preserving a converter after the route is actually dead and the backup route
  needs that turn or piece.

## Recipe: Primary-To-Backup Route Handoff

Trigger:

- The original plan is still emotionally tempting, but one of its required
  resources has changed: key HP, status, sleep, weather, hazards, PP, boosts,
  speed order, revealed coverage, or a blocker that did not fall.

Rule:

- Do not keep paying into a route after its price has become worse than the
  backup route. Handoff is correct when the old plan is live only with risky
  assumptions, while another plan now has fewer blockers or a clearer next
  step.

Source basis:

- Smogon's win-condition discussion frames the opening as identifying both
  sides' threats and then finding what can win after those threats are handled.
- Smogon's prediction/planning article says every plan needs a backup so one
  opposing Pokemon or style cannot foil the whole attempt.
- Smogon's long-term thinking and risk/reward articles both stress checking
  whether the battle is developing toward the plan and changing posture when it
  is not.

Check:

1. What did the primary route require?
2. Which requirement changed?
3. Is the old route still live, or only alive through a miss, crit, bad AI
   choice, or unverified mechanic?
4. Which backup route has the fewest remaining blockers now?
5. What piece must be preserved for the backup route?
6. What resource can be spent because it belonged only to the old route?
7. Does the handoff deny the opponent's current route, or does it just feel
   less bad than admitting the old route failed?

Decision rule:

- Keep primary when its blockers are still being removed and no irreplaceable
  resource has been compromised.
- Handoff when the primary route now requires unacceptable risk and the backup
  route has a concrete next move.
- Shorten the game when both long routes are damaged: prioritize immediate
  setup denial, forced KO, controlled sack, or revenge range over slow value.

Failure signs:

- Continuing to set up after the setup answer is paralyzed, asleep, or below
  range.
- Chasing the opening lead matchup while the boss's midgame ace has become the
  real threat.
- Preserving a piece for a route that is already dead while sacrificing the
  piece needed for the live backup route.
- Calling the backup "panic" without comparing its blockers to the old plan's
  blockers.

## Recipe: Risk And Worst Plausible Branch

Trigger:

- The best-looking move has a visible punish, or the matchup is unclear.

Rule:

- Compare the likely opponent move with the worst plausible opponent move. When
  we are ahead or stable, avoid irreversible catastrophe even if the average
  branch is tempting. When we are losing, accepting risk can be correct if
  safe play only loses slowly.

Source basis:

- Smogon's Risk/Reward guide separates immediate-risk analysis from the move
  that most hurts the overall strategy, then ties risk posture to matchup
  advantage.
- Smogon's Long Term Thinking guide says to notice when the battle is drifting
  away from the plan before the final collapse, and to take purposeful risks
  only when they pay toward the real route.
- Smogon's prediction guide frames prediction as information-backed guessing,
  not a reason to make stylish low-evidence plays.

Ask:

1. Is the punish legal and consistent with public information?
2. Is the opponent incentivized to choose it?
3. Is the damage or resource loss irreversible?
4. What do we gain if the punish does not happen?
5. Are we forced to accept the risk because all safer lines lose?

Posture labels:

```text
ahead:
  The current route wins if irreversible punish branches are avoided.

stable:
  Both sides have routes, but the low-risk line preserves ours and does not
  let the opponent's route become inevitable.

losing slowly:
  The low-risk line repeats the same bad cycle: recovery, hazards, setup,
  status, PP, or entry denial keeps improving the opponent's route.

forced risk:
  Every conservative line loses the same route map, so accept a narrower
  branch only if it creates a named winning route.
```

Decision rule:

- Ahead or stable: choose the line that preserves the route and avoids the
  irreversible branch, even if it wins slower.
- Behind or route collapsing: accept a calculated risk only if it creates or
  preserves a concrete route that safe play cannot.
- Unknown posture: first identify whether the current safe line is actually
  winning, drawing, or losing slowly.

Failure signs:

- Calling a play "safe" when it lets the boss's route become inevitable.
- Calling a play "high upside" without naming what route exists after it works.
- Playing around a tiny branch while ignoring the opponent's main route.
- Refusing necessary risk because the bad branch looks ugly, even though every
  safer move loses the same game more slowly.

## Recipe: Variance Budget Test

Trigger:

- A move choice depends on miss chance, critical hits, full paralysis, confusion,
  sleep turns, secondary effects, Quick Claw, Focus Band, damage rolls, or a
  prediction coinflip.

Rule:

- Price variance by posture. When the current route is winning or stable,
  reduce avoidable variance even if the safer move looks less powerful. When
  all non-variance lines lose, choose the line that gives the highest real
  chance to recover the route, then label it as forced risk instead of good
  control.

Source basis:

- Smogon's luck-control article makes the two-sided point: do not fish for luck
  when solid play can win, but in a lost position a 20% route is better than a
  0% route.
- The same article shows the winning-side mirror: once ahead, avoid letting an
  inaccurate move, critical hit, status secondary, or prediction coinflip become
  the only way the opponent gets back into the game.
- Smogon's situational analysis and prediction articles reinforce that move
  quality depends on the exact board, known information, and risk/reward, not
  on whether the move looked brave or conservative.

Ask:

1. Are we ahead, stable, losing slowly, or already forced into risk?
2. Which random branch can steal the route: miss, crit, full paralysis, Focus
   Band, Quick Claw, sleep turn, confusion, damage roll, or secondary effect?
3. Can another move keep the same route while removing that branch?
4. If not, what is the highest-probability comeback branch that still creates a
   named route?
5. Does the variance line expose an irreplaceable answer, or only spend a piece
   whose job is already gone?
6. What next state exists if the variance goes our way?
7. If the variance fails, was the decision bad, or was the route already lost?

Decision rule:

- Ahead or stable: prefer the move that keeps the route alive through the common
  miss/crit/status/item branch, even if it wins one turn slower.
- Losing slowly: accept variance only when the safe line preserves no realistic
  route and the high-variance line has a concrete follow-up.
- Forced risk: name the exact chance branch and the route it opens; do not
  rewrite the result later as proof the line was clean.
- If a move wins only by hoping for a critical hit, demote it below any line
  that wins through switching, recovery, hazard pressure, status assignment, or
  opponent accuracy first.

Failure signs:

- Calling a low-accuracy or crit-fishing move "the play" without proving all
  steadier routes fail.
- Using a 100% accurate weaker move when the stronger inaccurate move is the
  only route that can prevent an immediate loss.
- Setting up for many turns while each turn gives the boss another crit,
  status, Quick Claw, or Focus Band chance to break the route.
- Reviewing a win as correct only because the lucky branch happened.
- Reviewing a loss as bad luck when a lower-variance route was available.

## Recipe: Preservation Pivot Test

Trigger:

- The active Pokemon has useful long-game tools, but the opponent has a public
  punish that may remove it before those tools convert.

Rule:

- Switching is correct when it preserves a live role and creates a cleaner next
  state. It is not correct merely because the matchup looks uncomfortable.

Ask:

   speed control, setup, Explosion, spin, or revenge killing?
2. Can the active's best move convert before the opponent's public punish?

4. Which pivot absorbs the public route best?
5. What branch punishes that pivot?
6. If the opponent predicts the switch, is that branch worse than staying in?
7. After the pivot, what is the next plan?

Decision rule:

- Pivot rises when the active's move is too slow and the active still has future

- Staying in rises when the active can KO, force recovery, set a decisive clock,

- Sacrifice rises only when the active's future role is gone or the sacrifice

## Recipe: Proactive Switch / Double-Switch Discipline

Trigger:

- The opponent is likely to switch, pivot, heal, or enter a known route piece;
  or a boss route sheet says the current exchange naturally hands the next turn
  to a specific threat.

Rule:

- A proactive switch is correct only when it enters the piece that improves the
  next route while the wrong branch remains survivable. Do not double-switch
  because "momentum" sounds good.

Source basis:

- Smogon's prediction article frames prediction as information-weighted risk,
  not blind guessing.
- Smogon's GSC introduction says progress often comes from exploiting switching
  patterns, forcing poor matchups, and improving position over many turns.
- Double-switch material is useful as an abstract tactic: predict the incoming
  answer and enter the piece that beats that answer. In boss fights, replace
  human bluff assumptions with roster, public state, and validated AI evidence.

Ask:

1. What makes the opponent switch or pivot: immediate KO threat, bad matchup,
   recovery pressure, hazard pressure, lock-in, AI scoring, or route need?
2. Which incoming piece is most incentive-compatible for the opponent?
3. What do we gain if our proactive switch is right: KO threshold, setup window,
   hazard layer, status, forced Rest, trap, or safe entry?
4. What is the wrong branch if the opponent stays, attacks, sets up, heals, or
   chooses a different pivot?
5. Is that wrong branch survivable, or does it lose an irreplaceable answer?
6. Does the boss AI actually switch in this state, or are we importing human
   ladder assumptions?
7. If the proactive switch works, what is the next move? If there is no next
   route, the switch is probably cosmetic.

Decision rule:

- Proactive switching rises when the opponent's current active is strongly
  disincentivized from staying and the expected incoming piece is a real route
  blocker we can punish.
- Staying or using a broad punish rises when the opponent has several plausible
  pivots, the current move hits all branches acceptably, or the cost of being
  wrong is losing a key answer.
- Against boss AI, proactive switching requires local roster/AI evidence or a
  visible route necessity. Otherwise prefer route-preserving coverage of the
  worst plausible branch.

Failure signs:

- The explanation says "gain momentum" but cannot name the opponent's likely
  incoming route piece.
- The switch is only good if the opponent plays like a human ladder opponent.
- The wrong branch lets the boss set weather, boosts, hazards, sleep, or
  recovery that the new active cannot punish.
- The switch uses an irreplaceable answer as a prediction piece before its real
  matchup appears.

## Recipe: AI Move-Bias Switch Loop

Trigger:

- A boss or in-game AI has one move that strongly threatens the current active,
  and a teammate is immune to or heavily resists that move.

Rule:

- A switch loop is real only when the AI is likely to choose the punishable
  move, both sides of the loop survive their assigned entries, and the loop
  creates progress: PP depletion, safe setup, weather burn, recovery forcing,
  status avoidance, or clean entry. A single safe switch-in is not enough.

Source basis:

- Smogon's Battle Maison advice says AI KO preference can make switch-stalling
  possible when one move KOs the current Pokemon and a teammate blanks that
  move, but it warns that repeated resisted hits, secondary effects, and the
  foe's second coverage move can still break the line.
- Local boss AI is not vanilla Maison AI. Gym Leader Lab uses public-info KO
  pressure, weighted move selection, switch heuristics, and plan bias. Treat
  Maison behavior as a hypothesis until local source or trace evidence supports
  the exact state.

Ask:

1. Which boss move is expected, and why: KO pressure, strongest scored move,
   speed control, plan bias, or trace evidence?
2. Which teammate blanks or resists it, and what boss move threatens that
   teammate next?
3. Can the original active re-enter on that second move, or does the loop stop
   after one pivot?
4. What does the loop gain: PP, weather turns, poison turns, setup turn,
   recovery timing, or a safer entry?
5. What breaks the loop: hazards, Pursuit, trapping, secondary burn/freeze,
   crit, speed drop, accuracy drop, prediction-weighted switch, or low PP?
6. Is the loop still correct if the AI chooses its second-best move under the
   local weighted dice?

Decision rule:

- Use the loop when it survives the AI's likely and second-plausible moves and
  the gained clock matters to a route.
- Use one pivot, then attack or set up, when the first switch creates the entry
  but repeated switching only invites chip or secondary effects.
- Do not loop when hazards, status, weather, Pursuit, or coverage make the
  "safe" switch lose the answer needed later.
- Against local bosses, require current source, trace, or route-sheet evidence
  before calling the AI move predictable enough to loop.

Failure signs:

- The explanation says "switch to the resist" but cannot name the boss's next
  best coverage move.
- The loop drains PP that is not the real bottleneck.
- Repeated switching spends the same irreplaceable answer the route needs
  later.
- The plan imports Maison KO-bias into Gym Leader Lab without checking local
  AI evidence.

## Recipe: Prediction-Minimizing Pressure

Trigger:

- A turn looks like it requires a prediction, but our team has multiple pieces
  that pressure the same boss answer or defensive stabilizer.

Rule:

- Prefer moves that improve the route against both the stay-in branch and the
  expected answer. A strong plan makes the opponent's normal responses concede
  something: HP, status, recovery, hazards, safe entry, or removal of a blocker.

Source basis:

- Stathakis's DPP "A Greek RMT" argues that offense can reduce prediction by
  overlapping threats against the same defensive answers, then spending one
  attacker to weaken the shared blocker so the next attacker inherits progress.
  The exact team and DPP mechanics are not the transferable lesson; the
  transferable lesson is route pressure that makes reasonable responses
  expensive.

Ask:

1. Which opposing piece is the shared blocker or stabilizer?
2. Which of our pieces pressure that same blocker?
3. What happens if the opponent stays in?
4. What happens if the opponent uses the blocker?
5. Which teammate inherits the damage, status, or forced recovery?
6. Does staying in give the opponent setup, hazards, sleep, weather, or a
   recovery loop that is worse than the pressure gained?

Decision rule:

- Broad pressure rises when the move advances the route against multiple
  plausible branches.
- A narrow prediction rises only when the broad move fails to change the route
  and the read has a clear, survivable downside.
- Sacrifice rises when the active piece's route job is to put the shared
  blocker into range for the next piece.
- Switching rises when staying in spends an irreplaceable answer or gives the
  boss a stronger conversion route than the pressure earns.

Failure signs:

- The explanation says "predict the switch" but cannot name the shared blocker
  and inheriting teammate.
- The active attacker is preserved forever while the shared blocker remains
  healthy.
- A sacrifice damages something valuable but not the blocker the route needs.
- The line imports a human bluff assumption into a boss fight without local AI
  evidence.

## Recipe: Check Durability Ledger

Trigger:

- A Pokemon is being called a check, counter, wall, pivot, or answer to a live
  boss route.

Rule:

- Classify the answer by how it enters and how many times it still works from
  the current state. Do not let a full-HP theory label survive unchanged after
  hazards, status, sleep, weather, item loss, revealed coverage, or PP pressure.

Source basis:

- Smogon's checks/counters article distinguishes free-entry checks from
  manual-switch counters, and emphasizes set dependence.
- Smogon's introductory guide makes the same practical point: a check may win a
  one-on-one after entry, while a counter can switch into the attack and still
  win.
- Pivot material adds the missing battle-use detail: a pivot's value is not
  only taking a hit, but taking it while preserving role and creating a better
  next state.

Ledger:

```text
Threat:
  current route:
  confirmed moves:
  plausible coverage:

Answer:
  role label: counter / free-entry check / revenge check / one-time emergency /
              no longer answer
  can hard switch now:
  survives after hazards/weather/status:
  wins if entering free:
  how many entries remain:
  critical HP/PP/status:
  what revealed info breaks the label:
  what route opens if this answer is spent:
```

Decision rule:

- Hard switch only with a current counter or a one-time emergency answer whose
  spend is justified by the route it preserves.
- Preserve a free-entry check until a sack, pivot, forced lock, recovery turn,
  or KO gives it clean entry.
- Use a pivot when it creates that clean entry without losing its own required
  role. Do not call a switch progress if the next Pokemon enters at the cost of
  the only later answer.
- Revenge with a revenge check only after priority, Quick Claw, weather, and
  residual damage are priced.
- Stop calling a Pokemon an answer once the route requires a miss, crit,
  impossible damage roll, or unverified mechanic.

Failure signs:

- "This walls it" without checking the revealed coverage and current HP.
- Treating a one-time switch-in as a repeatable counter.
- Spending a free-entry check by hard-switching it through hazards/status.
- Preserving a piece for a threat that is already dead while losing the answer
  to the live threat.
- Counting a romhack type/passive interaction from vanilla memory.

## Recipe: Entry Creation Ladder

Trigger:

- The best route piece does not want to hard-switch into the current attack,
  status, hazard, trap, or setup branch.

Rule:

- Do not ask only "what is the best answer?" Ask how that answer enters. A
  free-entry check is not a counter until the board creates its entry.

Source basis:

- Smogon's pivot article frames pivots as Pokemon that switch in and out
  repeatedly to create momentum for teammates. The transferable lesson is not
  the later-generation move U-turn or Volt Switch by itself; it is controlled
  entry creation.
- The same article highlights slow U-turn, Volt Switch, defensive switching,
  and blanket checking as ways to bring wallbreakers or answers in without
  taking damage. In Gym Leader Lab, the equivalent entry can also come from a
  forced recovery turn, recharge turn, lock-in, phaze, KO, Encore, sacrifice,
  or boss AI commitment.

Use this ladder:

```text
1. Hard switch:
   legal only if the answer survives the current branch and still performs its
   later role.

2. Soft pivot:
   switch through a piece that absorbs the branch and does not lose a unique
   future job.

3. Forced turn:
   create entry on the opponent's recovery, recharge, locked move, weak move,
   setup denial, phaze, Encore, or miss-punish turn.

4. Controlled sack:
   spend a piece with no unique remaining job to enter the route piece safely.

5. Shorten the route:
   if no safe entry exists, use damage, status, Explosion, or sacrifice to make
   the current board winnable without that perfect entry.
```

Ask:

1. Which Pokemon wins or stabilizes if it enters safely?
2. What branch makes hard-switching it bad?
3. Which lower-value piece can absorb that branch?
4. Can we create a forced turn before needing the answer?
5. If we sack, what job does the sacrificed piece lose?
6. If no entry exists, what shorter route remains?
7. Does the entry plan rely on local AI, type-passive, item, or damage evidence
   that is not yet checked?

Decision rule:

- Hard switch rises when the answer is a current counter, not merely a
  free-entry check.
- Soft pivot rises when the pivot survives and the resulting entry improves a
  route immediately.
- Forced entry rises when the opponent's move is committed, weak, or
  exploitable by public state.
- Sack rises only when the sacrificed piece is genuinely lower value than the
  safe entry created.
- Shorten the route when every entry plan spends the same irreplaceable piece
  the plan is supposed to preserve.

Failure signs:

- Calling a Pokemon an answer without stating how it enters.
- Hard-switching a free-entry check through the exact hit or status that makes
  it stop checking later.
- Pivoting with the only answer to a different live route.
- Sacking for "momentum" when the incoming Pokemon does not create immediate
  route progress.
- Waiting for a perfect entry while the boss accumulates hazards, weather,
  screens, boosts, poison, or recovery turns.

## Recipe: Post-Faint Replacement Test

Trigger:

- One of our Pokemon faints, an opposing Pokemon faints, or a forced switch
  gives a clean replacement choice before the next turn.

Rule:

- A free switch is a resource. Spend it on the piece whose entry improves the
  current route or denies the boss's next route, not automatically on the
  Pokemon with the best visible one-on-one matchup.

Source basis:

- Smogon's checks/counters article defines a free switch as entry without the
  opponent immediately acting, and separates free-entry checks from counters
  that can manually switch into a hit.
- Pivot material frames safe entry as a way to bring in wallbreakers, setup
  pieces, or defensive answers without taking the damage that would make their
  job fail.
- Smogon's Battle Maison AI notes add a useful in-game warning: after a KO, AI
  replacement choice may follow its own scoring policy rather than human-style
  matchup sense. Treat the replacement as predictable only when the current
  game's source, trace, or observed behavior supports that policy.

Ask:

1. What boss Pokemon or route is most likely to come in next?
2. Which of our pieces needs a free entry most badly?
3. If we bring in the obvious attacker, what boss route gets a free answer,
   setup turn, recovery turn, weather turn, or priority revenge?
4. If we bring in the defensive answer now, are we spending the only answer
   before its real target appears?
5. Does this entry reveal a key move or item before its lure value matters?
6. What happens if the boss uses the worst route move, not the likely move?
7. What is the next action after entry: KO, force Rest, set hazard, phaze,
   recover, scout, status, or preserve?
8. Is the expected replacement based on local AI evidence, or on a human
   matchup guess?

Decision rule:

- Bring in the converter when its blockers are gone or the entry forces the
  boss to answer immediately.
- Bring in the answer when the next boss route can become irreversible before
  another clean entry appears.
- Bring in a pivot when it covers multiple likely replacements and keeps the
  irreplaceable piece hidden or healthy.
- Bring in a lower-value piece when the free switch is needed to scout the
  boss's replacement without exposing the actual answer.
- Do not use a free switch to take a KO if the resulting boss entry creates a
  stronger route than the KO removed.
- If local replacement policy is unknown, choose an entry that covers the
  high-severity replacement family, not just the one a human would send.

Failure signs:

- "Send in the strongest attacker" without naming the boss's next entry.
- Predicting the boss replacement from human matchup intuition when local AI
  source or trace evidence points elsewhere.
- Using a free entry on a piece that could have hard-switched safely anyway,
  while a free-entry-only check remains stranded.
- Revealing the lure, coverage, or item before the route blocker appears.
- Letting the boss enter a setup, weather, recovery, or priority route because
  the replacement choice only solved the previous matchup.

## Recipe: Converter Support Ladder

Trigger:

- A Pokemon looks like the eventual winner, but its blockers, entry condition,
  or support requirements are not solved yet.

Rule:

- Do not cash the converter just because it is strong. Build the board where
  its remaining attack, setup turn, recovery loop, priority move, or trade
  actually wins. A support move is progress only if it completes a missing line
  in the converter ledger.

Source basis:

- Smogon's Long Term Thinking article says to save a sweeper until its checks
  have been identified and weakened, then give it safe entry instead of trying
  to win immediately.
- Smogon's win-condition material frames this as naming what can win after the
  blockers are removed.
- Reviewed GSC games show the same pattern with Marowak, Jolteon, and other
  converters: early support can look inactive while it is actually making the
  final route safer.

Ledger:

```text
converter:
required entry state:
current blockers:
support job 1:
support job 2:
support job 3:
first converter entry should:
backup converter:
event that proves this route is dead:
```

Decision rule:

- Preserve the converter when a later entry is still live and the current turn
  only risks HP, status, PP, or item resources without solving a blocker.
- Use the converter when it can remove a named blocker, force a reset the team
  can exploit, or start a sequence the opponent can no longer interrupt.
- Switch to a backup route when the support jobs can no longer be completed or
  another converter now has fewer blockers.
- Stop preserving the converter once its route is solved; at that point, cash
  it before the opponent rebuilds a reset, status, or priority branch.

Boss application:

- In Gym Leader Lab, this is how to plan around setup sweepers, weather
  abusers, bulky Rest users, priority cleaners, and one-time trade users. For a
  boss fight, turn-1 planning should name not only "who wins later," but what
  support jobs must be completed before that Pokemon enters.

## Recipe: Move Choice As "So What?"

Trigger:

- A move sounds active: attack, Toxic, Spikes, setup, sleep, phaze, scout, or
  sacrifice.

Rule:

- After choosing the move, answer what concrete route is better next turn.

Good answers:

- "This forces Rest, giving our setup user a safe entry."
- "This preserves the only answer to the opponent's setup route."
- "This hazard layer turns future phazing from delay into progress."
- "This Explosion removes the blocker to our endgame, and the lost role is no

Bad answers:

- "It pressures them."
- "Spikes are good."
- "Sleep gives free turns."
- "It does super effective damage."

## Recipe: Foresight Ladder Test

Trigger:

- A move wins the visible exchange, adds chip, lands status, sets support, or
  preserves a Pokemon, but the later route is not yet named.

Rule:

- A good move should usually name at least two future route steps: what the
  move changes next turn, and what that next turn is trying to unlock. Borat's
  GSC guide separates short prediction from long-term foresight; the practical
  translation is that every apparently useful move must answer "what opening
  am I building toward?"

Ask:

1. If this move works, what exact board do we want next turn?
2. What does that board unlock: forced Rest, safe entry, KO threshold, phaze
   damage, PP pressure, setup, item spend, recovery denial, or endgame cleanup?
3. Which opponent route gets easier if we take this local value?
4. If we switch, status, set hazards, attack, recover, or sacrifice instead,
   which line has the clearest next two steps?
5. Is this move only improving the scoreline, or is it improving the win route?
6. What would make the route ladder dead after this move resolves?

Decision rule:

- Choose the locally useful move when its next two steps improve a concrete
  route and the opponent's best public branch does not erase that progress.
- Choose positioning, preservation, or a different support move when damage or
  status lacks a follow-up and the alternative creates cleaner entry or route
  denial.
- Take the extra attack or sacrifice only when the gained hit is guaranteed to
  matter to the route. Otherwise preserve or reposition.
- If an immediate threat must be denied, the ladder can be short: stop the live
  route now, then re-score from the new state.

Failure signs:

- The explanation says "good chip," "momentum," or "pressure" without naming
  the next board.
- The move wins turn 1 but gives the boss's real route a free entrance.
- A sacrifice buys one attack that does not force a KO, Rest, recovery, phaze,
  PP spend, or safe entry.
- The plan keeps preserving a Pokemon after its route is dead, or spends it
  before its route has been prepared.

## Recipe: Standard-Function Denial Test

Trigger:

- The opposing Pokemon is not only attacking. It is trying to perform a team
  function: recovery, hazards, screen support, status, trapping, phazing, item
  removal, setup support, or sacrifice pressure.

Rule:

- Identify the function that makes the opposing route work, then choose the
  move that denies that function while preserving the piece needed for the next
  route. Raw damage is best only when it actually removes or forces the
  function before it matters.

Source basis:

- Smogon stallbreaker material frames defensive disruption around stopping
  common defensive structures from doing their usual jobs: healing, spreading
  status, using passive damage, protecting key items, or recovering through
  weak attacks.
- This does not transfer as a species or moveset rule. Transfer it as a
  question: "what standard function is this opponent trying to execute, and
  which action prevents it?"

Ask:

1. What is this Pokemon's current job for the opponent: heal, set hazards,
   screen, status, trap, scout, phaze, spin, explode, setup, or absorb damage?
2. If it performs that job, which later route becomes harder to stop?
3. Does direct damage remove the function before it happens?
4. Is a control move, status, pivot, item denial, setup denial, or sacrifice a
   cleaner answer to the function than raw damage?
5. Does the denial move expose our only answer to the piece that inherits the
   board afterward?
6. What local mechanic, AI, type-passive, item, or damage fact is needed before
   the denial label is trusted?

Decision rule:

- Attack rises when it KOs, forces recovery that can be punished, or prevents
  the support action from resolving.
- Control rises when the opponent survives direct damage but loses the route if
  healing, status, screens, hazards, or setup are blocked.
- Pivoting rises when the current active cannot deny the function without
  losing an irreplaceable later role.
- Sacrifice rises only when the sacrificed piece has finished its job and the
  denial opens a named route.

Failure signs:

- The explanation says "break the wall" without naming what the wall is doing.
- The move does damage, but the opponent still heals, sets the layer, screens,
  traps, phazes, or trades in the way its team wanted.
- The player stops the support Pokemon while spending the only answer to the
  supported route.
- The line relies on a Taunt-like, type, passive, item, or AI interaction that
  has not been checked in the romhack profile.

## Recipe: Reveal Timing / Lure Resource Test

Trigger:

- A move, item, set detail, unusual coverage option, one-time resource, or
  route-specific tech can remove a blocker or force a decisive misalignment.

Rule:

- Reveal the tech when it changes the route, not merely when it is available.
  A good reveal either removes the blocker to a named win condition, forces a
  recovery/status/switch state the team can exploit, or prevents the boss's
  immediate route. It should also leave the user with a useful job if the exact
  target never appears.
- Traps and lures need a beneficiary. Name the Pokemon or route that improves
  after the blocker is gone, then name the next blocker before calling the
  trade good.

Source basis:

- Smogon's lure articles define a lure by the route blocker it draws in and
  removes, not by the novelty of the set.
- Smogon's surprise article stresses that the surprise should fit the team's
  actual plan.
- Smogon's prediction article treats hidden information as valuable only when
  the player has enough evidence and the risk/reward is favorable.

Check:

1. What exact blocker is this reveal meant to remove or cripple?
2. What win condition improves after that blocker is gone?
3. If we reveal now, can the opponent still save the blocker or pivot to a
   different answer?
4. If we wait, can the reveal user stay healthy enough to perform the job?
5. Does the reveal have useful value if the intended target never appears?
6. Is this a human-opponent assumption, or does local boss AI evidence support
   the same branch?
7. Does the reveal depend on a romhack type, passive, item, or damage claim
   that needs local evidence?

Decision rule:

- Reveal rises when the target is present, the effect changes a route, and
  delaying risks losing the chance.
- Holding rises when the target is absent, the current hit would be generic
  chip, or the opponent can scout/avoid the reveal without losing anything.
- Do not call a reveal good because it is unexpected. Call it good because it
  changes the route map.

Boss transfer:

- Against fixed AI, "lure" often means timing a limited answer rather than
  tricking a human. Do not assume the boss changes decisions because it has or
  lacks knowledge unless the AI behavior is locally verified.
- The useful translation is: keep the route-specific move, item, or sacrifice
  for the Pokemon whose removal actually opens the fight, unless using it now
  prevents a worse immediate route.

Failure signs:

- The surprise is revealed into the wrong target because it is flashy.
- The move removes a Pokemon but not a route blocker.
- The trap or lure removes the right target, but the beneficiary is already
  gone, too low, status-locked, or still blocked by the next answer.
- The set has no useful job if the intended target does not appear.
- The explanation relies on human bluffing in an AI boss fight without local AI
  evidence.

## Recipe: Revealed Function Reclassification

Trigger:

- A familiar Pokemon reveals a move, damage threshold, screen, trap, Haze,
  phazing, spin, recovery loop, PP pattern, or lock/ramp route that contradicts
  the role you were assigning from species alone.

Rule:

- Re-label the Pokemon by the function it has publicly shown. A wall with
  demonstrated damage is not free setup bait; a support Pokemon that completed
  its job before fainting may already have converted value; a passive-looking
  move that strongly points to a lock route means preserve the answer while
  checking whether the companion move is proven.

Check:

1. What was the old species-role assumption?
2. What exact function has now been revealed?

## Recipe: Immediate Conversion / KO Threshold Test

Trigger:

- A direct attack may KO, force recovery, stop setup, prevent healing, or remove
  the active route before the opponent acts.

Rule:

- Damage is valuable when it changes the state. If public damage evidence says
  an attack removes the active threat or crosses a decisive threshold, it can
  dominate slower plans such as status, setup, scouting, or preservation.

Ask:

1. Does the attack KO or force a specific recovery/switch line?
2. If the target survives, what can it do: recover, KO back, status, set up,
   phaze, spin, or sacrifice?
3. Does a slower move give the opponent a turn to erase the progress?
4. Is the attacker faster, or does it survive the hit before attacking?
5. What happens after the KO: which opposing Pokemon comes in, and are we
   exposing an irreplaceable piece?
6. What public damage evidence supports the threshold?
7. What information would flip the answer away from attacking?

Decision rule:

- Attack rises when it removes the active route before recovery or retaliation
  matters.
- Status or setup rises when damage is only chip and the control move changes a
  longer route.
- Switching rises when the attack fails to convert and the active is still
  needed as an answer.
- Switching falls when the proposed pivot is punished by the same branch and
  the active attack creates more route pressure from the current HP range.

Damage evidence ladder:

- `type_chart_label`: claim only from the active mechanics profile. Vanilla GSC
  and the romhack are separate charts. For the romhack, check
  `data/types/type_matchups.asm` and
  `docs/agent_navigation/gen2_vs_modern_mechanics.md`; the 15 local type-chart
  edits are known facts, not hypotheticals.
- `passive_adjusted_result`: claim only after checking relevant type passives in
  `engine/battle/type_passive_damage_mods.asm` or a source-derived reference.
  Do not let a chart word stand in for passive-adjusted damage.
  See `romhack_deltas/type_passive_route_impacts.md` for the move-choice
  triage layer.
- `final_damage_range`: claim from a public benchmark fixture, trusted local
  calc, source-derived table, or debugger/emulator observation. Source code is
  authority for general mechanics; a validated debugger trace is authority for
  the exact battle-state result. A disagreement means a source-version or
  tooling mismatch to investigate.
- `strategic_label`: call a move best, acceptable, wrong, or catastrophic only
  after the range is tied to a route: KO, forced recovery, setup denial, hazard
  threshold, survival threshold, PP clock, or safe entry.

Use this wording discipline:

- "Base chart says..." for chart-only evidence.
- "Romhack source/docs say..." for checked local deltas.
- "Public fixture/debugger range says..." for KO or survival thresholds.
- "Therefore this move changes the route because..." for strategic labels.

Failure signs:

- Saying "super effective," "resisted," "immune," or "neutral" without naming
  the mechanics profile and evidence source.
- Treating a type word as a KO claim.
- Treating a KO range as a full move label without checking speed, retaliation,
  recovery, hazards, and next-entry consequences.
- Importing vanilla Steel/Ghost/Dark behavior into the romhack. In this hack,
  Dark into Steel is neutral and Ghost into Steel has no effect.

Trigger:

  convert our apparently strong attack into a lost route.

Rule:

- Do not call direct damage correct until the retaliation branch is priced. A
  heavy hit is still good when it KOs first, when the punishment cannot
  legally resolve, when the attacker is expendable and opens a stronger route,
  or when the alternative lets the opponent's route become worse.

Source basis:

- Smogon's risk/reward material says to compare both the likely move and the
  move that most damages the overall strategy.
- Smogon's prediction material treats move choice as information-backed risk,
  not as automatic avoidance of every punish.
- Local source says Mirror Coat only answers special-category damage and works
  only after the opponent has already acted that turn:
  `engine/battle/move_effects/mirror_coat.asm` and
  `docs/agent_navigation/gen2_vs_modern_mechanics.md`.
- Local boss AI source treats Counter/Mirror Coat as a public revealed-move
  trade read for mid/late bosses, not current-turn mind-reading.
- Wobbuffet-style expert examples are strongest because trapping, Encore, or
  revealed category reduces the target's freedom. A standalone boss Mirror Coat
  user without that lock is a branch to price, not a proof that special damage
  is forbidden.

Ask:

1. Which retaliation move is public or plausible?
2. What category, timing, target, and damage condition does it require?
3. Does our attack KO before the retaliation user can act?
4. If the target survives, does retaliation remove an irreplaceable piece?
5. If we avoid the punish with a physical move, status, pivot, or sacrifice,
   does that line still change the route?
6. Is the retaliation move likely from local AI evidence, or only severe?
7. What happens if the opponent attacks instead of using the punish move?
8. Has the relevant attacking category actually been revealed to the boss?
9. Is the retaliation user backed by trapping, Encore, choice lock, or another
   condition that forces the attacking category?

Decision rule:

- Attack rises when the hit KOs, the retaliation condition is impossible, or
  losing the attacker creates a forced route that is worth the trade.
- Physical or non-damaging pressure rises when it avoids the retaliation branch
  while still forcing recovery, status, a KO threshold, or safe entry.
- Pivoting rises when the current attacker is irreplaceable and the pivot
  survives the opponent's coverage if they attack instead.
- Sacrifice rises when it creates clean entry for the real answer and the sack
  no longer blocks a later route.
- Avoiding retaliation falls when the safer-looking line gives the opponent a
  free setup, recovery, weather, screen, or anchor turn.

Failure signs:

- "Use the strongest special move" into a known Mirror Coat user without KO,

- "Avoid Mirror Coat" by switching to a piece that loses to Surf, Earthquake,
  Blizzard, setup, or the next boss route.
- Treating a severe punish as guaranteed AI behavior without source or trace
  support.
- Ignoring retaliation because the attack is type-advantaged.

## Recipe: Protection State Test

Trigger:

- Substitute, Protect, Detect, Endure, Focus Band, or another block/survival
  state can make the selected move fail to affect the real target this turn.

Rule:

- Before recommending a KO, status, Explosion, Focus Punch, sleep, trap, or
  setup line, ask whether the move actually reaches the target and whether the
  protected turn gives either side route value. Protection is progress only if
  it creates a free turn, scouts decisive information, preserves a route, burns
  scarce PP, or makes the opponent spend HP/tempo into a barrier.

Source basis:

- Smogon's Substitute analysis frames Substitute as a way to block status,
  scout, create free turns, protect Focus Punch/setup, and sometimes tax PP.
- Reviewed GSC games show Substitute can be both protection and self-tax: it is
  strong when it creates a protected converter turn, weak when the opponent can
  break it repeatedly while staying healthy.
- Local move data includes `Substitute`, `Protect`, `Detect`, and `Endure`;
  local priority data gives Protect/Endure priority 3; local boss data includes
  Protect users and Focus Band branches.

Ask:

1. Is a Substitute, Protect, Endure, Focus Band, screen, or similar protection
   branch already active or strongly incentivized?
2. Does the planned move affect the real Pokemon, only the barrier, or nothing?
3. If the opponent protects, what do they gain: Leftovers, poison/weather turn,
   scout, PP drain, recharge/lock reset, Explosion dodge, or setup safety?
4. If we break Substitute, what is the next forced state?
5. If we use Substitute/Protect, does the protected turn create a route or only
   spend HP/PP while the boss improves?
6. What move ignores or punishes the protection: phazing, Haze, Encore, setup,
   hazard, recovery denial, multi-hit, status after Sub breaks, or switching?
7. What is the follow-up if Focus Band or Endure leaves the target alive?

Decision rule:

- Break or deny protection when the barrier would give the opponent setup,
  Focus Punch, recovery, hazard, or status tempo.
- Use protection when it scouts a decisive branch, blocks a crippling status,
  preserves an irreplaceable piece, or creates a concrete follow-up.
- Avoid status, sleep, trap, Explosion, or Focus Punch lines when the most
  plausible protection branch makes them miss the real target and the follow-up
  is not planned.
- Treat Focus Band / Endure survival as a live branch when the fight depends on

  Explosion pressure; do not spend a one-time trade into a plausible block
  branch without a follow-up.
- Morty, Bruno, Lance, and other Focus Band users can turn "KO and move on"
  into "survives and uses one more control, priority, sleep, or setup turn."

## Recipe: Locked-Move Commitment Test

Trigger:

- A move locks the user into a future sequence or creates a forced follow-up:
  Outrage, Thrash, Petal Dance, Rollout, Bide, Hyper Beam recharge, Encore, or a
  similar commitment.

Rule:

- A locked move is a route commitment, not just a damage button. Use it when the
  forced sequence wins or when the opponent's best pivots are already removed,
  trapped, too low, or unable to punish. Against it, look for the pivot, phaze,
  Haze, sacrifice, status, or revenge route that exploits the commitment.

Source basis:

- Smogon's Move Restriction Guide separates move selection, execution, success,
  and PP loss. That matters because rampage and momentum moves are usually
  selected once but execute over later turns, recharge turns are not move
  executions, and failed or missed executed moves can still consume PP or
  maintain a lock depending on the mechanic.
- Do not transfer those exact DPP mechanics blindly into Gym Leader Lab. Use
  the distinction as the question to ask, then verify the local source for
  Rollout, Outrage, Encore, Disable, Taunt, Choice items, recharge, and PP.

Ask:

1. How many turns are forced, and what happens at the end: confusion, recharge,
   vulnerability, or free switch?
2. Was the move selected, executed, successful, or merely attempted?
3. Did PP actually get spent, and does this lock / restriction care about PP?
4. Which opposing pieces can enter during the lock and change the route?
5. Does the locked move cross a KO threshold, or does it merely hand the
   opponent a predictable board?
6. Can hazards, phazing, Haze, Protect, recovery, or sacrifice turn the lock
   against the user?
7. Is the user still needed after the locked sequence ends?
8. What bad-luck or item branch changes the commitment, such as Quick Claw,
   Focus Band, miss, or status cure?
9. If the lock starts now, what is the planned board when it ends?

Decision rule:

- Locking in rises when the opponent's remaining answers cannot exploit the
  sequence and the damage converts into a named win route.
- Holding the move rises when the opponent still has a healthy pivot, phazer,
  Haze user, revenge killer, or sacrifice that makes the forced sequence bad.
- Against a locked opponent, stop the old plan and build the punish sequence
  around the fact that their next action is constrained.
- Treat selection, execution, success, and PP as separate evidence fields
  before using a move-restriction claim to justify a line.

Romhack application:

  before confusion. The hack also has an Outrage category exception for Dragon
  users and Dragon Dance boosts the user's currently higher offensive stat plus
  Speed, so damage/category claims need local evidence.
- Local Encore, Choice item, Disable, Taunt, Rollout, recharge, and PP claims
  must cite source or fixture behavior when they change the move choice.

## Recipe: Status Absorber Assignment

Trigger:

- The opponent can land sleep, paralysis, poison, burn, confusion, or another
  control effect, and the current plan requires deciding whether to take it,
  dodge it, deny it, or spend a different piece.

Rule:

- A status absorber is not just the lowest-value Pokemon. It is the Pokemon
  whose remaining jobs still work after taking that specific status, or whose
  unique job is already gone. Assign the absorber before the status lands; if
  the only possible target is irreplaceable, treat the status move as a route
  threat to deny instead of a nuisance to accept.

Source basis:

- GSC status material treats sleep, paralysis, and poison as route-changing
  effects, not generic bad luck. Sleep Talk and Rest can make some sturdy
  Pokemon poor sleep targets, while paralysis hurts fast defensive and revenge
  routes much more than slow routes.
- GSC Spikes material warns that status support works best when aimed at
  Pokemon that actually care about that status; the same logic applies on
  defense, where a good absorber is the piece least damaged in route terms.
- Later-generation status material names the "status absorber" idea directly:
  a team can intentionally route predicted status into a Pokemon that is not
  meaningfully harmed by it. Translate the concept back through GSC and
  romhack mechanics before applying it.

Ask:

1. Which status is being assigned: sleep, paralysis, poison, burn, confusion,
   trap, or a compound clock?
2. Which future job would each possible target still need to perform?
3. Does the status break that job by removing Speed, turns awake, HP clock,
   damage output, switch freedom, recovery timing, or PP?
4. Is there a cure, item, RestTalk route, immunity, already-used clause, or
   statused piece that changes the cost?
5. Does absorbing status give the opponent a better route anyway, such as
   rain, setup, recovery, screens, hazards, or a free switch?
6. Can direct pressure remove or punish the status user before the status
   matters?
7. If the assigned absorber is hit, what exact plan inherits the route?

Decision rule:

- Absorb status when the target's remaining role is redundant, already spent,
  or still functional while statused.
- Deny status when it would hit the only answer to a live opposing route, the

## Recipe: Status Reset Map

Trigger:

- A plan depends on sleep, paralysis, poison, burn, confusion, trap status, or
  another control effect staying relevant across future turns.

Rule:

- Before calling status progress, map how the status can be reset, prevented,
  or made irrelevant. Status is only real progress if it creates value before
  Rest, Heal Bell, Full Heal, Full Restore, Safeguard, switching, type
  immunity, item cure, or a status-indifferent route erases it.

Source basis:

- GSC status material explicitly warns that Heal Bell and Rest change the value
  of status, and that curing sleep can reopen the opponent's ability to use a
  sleep move again.
- GSC Spikes material says poison and paralysis are strongest when paired with
  switch pressure, phazing, forced Rest, or setup conversion. Status without a
  conversion plan may be undone before it matters.
- Local boss data makes this a practical Gym Leader Lab issue:
  `data/trainers/attributes.asm` gives some bosses Full Heal or Full Restore,
  and `data/trainers/parties.asm` includes Safeguard, Rest, Recover,
  Moonlight, Morning Sun, Synthesis, and Milk Drink on important boss routes.

Ask:

1. What exact status or control effect is the route relying on?
2. What removes or prevents it: Rest, Heal Bell, Full Heal, Full Restore,
   Safeguard, item cure, switch reset, type immunity, passive, or clause?
3. Who owns the reset turn? Can we punish it with setup, hazards, phazing, a
   forced entry, PP pressure, or a KO threshold?
4. Does the status create value before the reset, such as forcing an item,
   forcing Rest, blocking Speed, denying setup, or opening a safe entry?
5. If the boss cures the target, is Sleep Clause, Toxic counter, confusion, or
   Perish/trap state reset in a way that helps or hurts us?
6. Is the status user still needed if the status plan gets reset?
7. What move becomes best if the status is prevented by Safeguard or a cure
   item?

Decision rule:

- Use status when it either cannot be reset cheaply, forces the reset at a
  punishable time, or creates immediate route value before being reset.
- Attack, pivot, phaze, set hazards, or set up when the boss can cure or
  prevent status for less cost than the status user spends.
- Preserve a status move when the current target is the wrong one to force the
  reset; use it later on the piece whose cure turn gives a real opening.
- After a cure or Safeguard, re-score from the new state. Do not keep using the
  same status plan into prevention.

Boss application:

- Misty's Full Heal can erase one status plan, so a paralysis or poison route
  should either force the item at a good time or convert before it is used.
- Koga, Champion, Red, and Blue Full Restore can undo both HP and status, so

- Karen Crobat and Blaine Ninetales can use Safeguard, which means sleep,
  Toxic, or Thunder Wave may need to be delayed, forced before Safeguard, or
  replaced by direct pressure while the prevention window is active.
- Rest users such as Koga Muk, Misty Quagsire, Sabrina Hypno, Will Slowbro, and
  Red Snorlax turn status into a timing question: can the user punish the Rest
  turns, PP, or entry created by the reset?

## Recipe: Clock Ownership Test

Trigger:

- Both sides can create timers or resets: Toxic, Spikes, trapping, confusion,
  recovery, Rest, Rapid Spin, Haze, phazing, or a fast cleaner.

Rule:

- A clock is good only for the side that can convert it. Before clicking a
  slow-control move, decide who benefits from the next three turns if the move
  lands, misses, or is answered.

Ask:

1. Who is actually losing HP, PP, switch freedom, recovery turns, or setup
   access after this move?
2. Can the target switch, Rest, heal, spin, Haze, phaze, or force us out before
   the timer matters?
3. Are we trapping the right Pokemon, or trapping ourselves into a bad damage
   race?
4. Does status plus hazards force a concrete Rest, KO, pivot, or setup window?
5. Which opposing piece becomes a cleaner if the clock damages our answer?
6. What direct attack, pivot, or sacrifice beats the clock plan?
7. If the clock starts, what is the next forced action?

Decision rule:

- Slow clocks rise when they damage an irreplaceable target, deny recovery or
  switching, and create a named follow-up.
- Direct pressure rises when the opponent's recovery, spin, Haze, or coverage
  means the clock helps them more than us.
- Pivoting rises when the current Pokemon would be trapped, poisoned, or chipped
  out of an irreplaceable role before the clock converts.
- Waiting rises only after the clock is already winning and the opponent's best
  response cannot create a new route. A no-effect or low-effect move is legal
  only when it preserves a contained loop while poison, hazards, weather turns,
  sleep turns, recoil, or PP do the real work.
- A contained loop is not automatically a winning loop. Before repeating it,
  name the endpoint: forced KO range, Rest timing, scarce PP, poison / hazard /
  weather / recoil clock, trap, switch denial, final trade, or a preserved
  converter. If the opponent can reset the chip while our converter is being
  spent, the line is a hold, not a win condition.

Boss application:

- Koga's local roster combines Spikes, Toxic, Spider Web, Rapid Spin, Haze,
  Curse, Rest, Moonlight, Pursuit, confusion, coverage, and fast cleanup. The
  central question is not "is Toxic good?" It is "who owns the poison/trap
  clock after this turn?"

## Recipe: Forced-Control Move Test

Trigger:

- Roar, Whirlwind, Perish Song, Haze, Encore, phazing, trapping, or another
  route-control move can deny the opponent's plan.

Rule:

- Spend a control move when it denies a live route. Do not spend it merely
  because the opponent looks scary or because the move is generally defensive.

Ask:

1. What route is the opponent actively threatening: boost, lock, setup pass,
   recovery loop, hazard removal, trapping, or endgame cleaner?
2. Does the control move actually deny or reset that route?
3. Does the user survive the public punish?
4. Does the control move work mechanically in this format?
5. What happens after the forced switch, countdown, Encore, or reset?
6. Is direct damage, status, pivoting, or sacrifice a stronger route answer?
7. Is the control move a finite resource whose PP or timing must be preserved?

Decision rule:

- Control rises when the opponent's route is live and direct damage or random
  disruption does not stop it.
- Control falls when the opponent has not yet created a real route, when the
  control user cannot survive, or when the move fails under current mechanics.
- After control succeeds, re-score. Do not assume the battle is solved just
  because the immediate route was denied.
- The best control turn is often the route boundary: after the opponent has
  committed to setup, recovery, sleep stabilization, trapping, or lock-in, but
  before that commitment has converted into an unmanageable board.

- Roar and Whirlwind also need a legal target pool. Once the target is on its
  last Pokemon, the move is no longer forced-switch control; hand off to
  damage, status, PP, recovery, setup, pivoting, or a trade.

## Recipe: Two-Sided Hazard War

Trigger:

- Both sides can set Spikes, remove Spikes, phaze, or punish the spinner.

Rule:

- Do not stop at "set hazards." A hazard plan needs a retention plan and a
  removal plan. The question is who wins the next spin turn and what the hazard
  layer changes if it stays.

Ask:

1. Who can set hazards, and are they still needed for another role?
2. Who can remove hazards, and can that spinner enter safely?
3. Can either side block, punish, status, or force out the spinner?
4. Does phazing create repeated grounded switch-ins, or are key targets Flying?
5. Does Explosion or sacrifice remove the spinner, setter, phazer, or wall that
   makes the hazard plan work?
6. Are the visible setter and the real spinner the same Pokemon, or is a
   reserve waiting to remove hazards after the forced switch?
7. If our layer is removed immediately, did the hazard turn still gain anything?
8. If we spin, what route are we preserving by removing the layer?
9. Does the spin turn also KO, force, or stabilize against the current active?

Decision rule:

- Hazard retention can be partial. Blocking the first spin is enough if the
  layer has already created the KO range, Rest cycle, switch tax, or endgame
  route we needed. If the spinner survives to remove the layer later, hand off
  immediately to the route the earlier hazard turns enabled.
- A spinblocker, trapper, or spinner punish is retention only if it can enter
  or act on the removal turn. A Ghost in the party is not enough if Rapid Spin
  resolves into a non-Ghost; damage into a spinner is not enough if the spinner
  clears the layer first and then Recovers or resets the route.
- Removing a spinner after it already spun is future control, not current
  retention. It is correct only if the post-trade setter/spinner map still
  favors the route we are trying to preserve.
- Rapid Spin can be a progress move when it clears hazards while also KOing,
  forcing, or preserving the spinner's future entries. Do not treat the spin
  turn as passive if the current active cannot punish it.
- Map support jobs across the team before attacking the visible support
  Pokemon. A Forretress, Qwilfish, or Cloyster in front of us may only be
  setting hazards or poisoning our setter; the actual spinner, phazer, Haze
  user, or trapper may be a reserve that enters after our forced response.

Failure signs:

- Stacking layers while the opponent has a free Rapid Spin turn.
- Spinning reflexively when attacking, phazing, or removing the setter would
  preserve the better route.
- Exploding the hazard setter before its Spikes, Spin, or defensive role has
  either converted or become irrelevant.
- Calling a sacrifice "hazard control" without saying whether it denied the
  current spin, future spins, the setter, or a separate route.
- Attacking the visible setter while a separate spinner enters next and clears
  the route for free.
- Counting hazard damage on Flying-type switch-ins in the romhack.

  Selfdestruct, Destiny Bond, Perish Song, Focus Band, sleep, a phazer's last

  resource. A trade is good only relative to the pieces and routes that remain

## Recipe: Controlled Sack For Clean Entry

Trigger:

- A win-condition Pokemon can make real progress if it enters safely, but a hard
  switch risks damage, status, sleep, trapping, or setup that breaks the route.

Rule:

- Do not try to win with every Pokemon alive when a smaller, cleaner win is
  safer. Letting a spent piece faint is correct only if the next entry creates
  a concrete route and the sacrificed piece has no irreplaceable job against
  the remaining boss roster.

Source basis:

- Smogon's Long Term Thinking article uses a sacrifice to give the actual
  cleaner a safe entry instead of gambling the advantage by preserving
  everything.
- Smogon's Risk/Reward article frames this by posture: when ahead, minimize
  irreversible risk until the game changer gets a reliable entry; when behind,
  accepting risk can be correct if safe play loses slowly.
- Smogon's prediction article treats bold plays as justified by information and
  risk/reward, not by wanting to look clever.

Check:

1. Which Pokemon enters after the sack, and what route improves immediately?
2. What live enemy route did the sacrificed piece still answer?
3. Is hard-switching the route piece worse than allowing the current piece to
   faint?
4. If the opponent refuses the KO and uses setup, recovery, status, or a switch,
   does the sack line still hold?
5. Does the sack force a locked move, recharge turn, recovery turn, or board
   state the next Pokemon can punish?
6. Would preserving the sacrificed Pokemon only improve the scoreline, or would
   it improve the win chance?

Boss applications:

- Bugsy: spending the only Scyther answer early is usually wrong, but spending
  an already-poisoned or no-longer-needed Ariados/Ledian answer can be right if
  it brings the Scyther answer in clean after Bugsy has committed to an attack
  instead of a support pass.
- Karen: a sack can be correct to enter safely against Houndoom after Sunny Day
  or Tyranitar after Dragon Dance, but only if the sacrificed piece is not the
  remaining answer to the other route.
- Lance: a sack can be correct when it catches Outrage or Hyper Beam and creates
  a punishable locked/recharge turn. It is wrong if that piece was still needed
  for Kingdra, Gyarados, or final Dragonite.

Failure signs:

- The sacrifice is only justified by "low HP."
- The next entry is not forced, safe, or route-improving.
- The opponent can use the sack turn to set up instead of KOing.
- The plan saves the scoreline less than it saves the win route.

## Recipe: PP Budget Ledger

Trigger:

- A long fight, Rest/recovery loop, hazard war, phazing loop, Encore/Disable
  line, or low-PP coverage move may decide the route.

Rule:

- Treat PP as material when one move is required to keep a route alive. Do not
  spend scarce recovery, phazing, Haze, high-power coverage, Rapid Spin, or
  setup-denial PP unless the turn changes a route or prevents an opposing route
  from becoming live.

Source basis:

- Smogon's GSC Spikes guide says some stall endgames become PP wars, and that
  Spikes helps only when the user's own side can avoid the same switch tax.
- Reviewed GSC games show long loops where the visible move result looks small
  but HP, PP, Rest cycles, spin timing, and preserved converters decide the
  eventual route.
- Local move data gives concrete PP limits for boss-relevant moves:
  `data/moves/moves.asm` has `Morning Sun`, `Synthesis`, and `Moonlight` at 5
  PP; `Hydro Pump`, `Thunder`, `Blizzard`, `Fire Blast`, `Rest`, `Protect`,
  `Softboiled`, and `Milk Drink` at 10 PP; `Recover`, `Roar`, and `Whirlwind`
  at 20 PP; `Haze` at 30 PP; and `Rapid Spin` at 40 PP.

Ledger:

```text
scarce move:
owner:
remaining PP:
route it protects:
route it attacks:
what forces it:
what lets us conserve it:
what happens at zero PP:
```

Ask:

1. Which PP count is the real clock: recovery, phazing, Haze, spin, coverage,
   setup, status, or protection?
2. Are we spending PP to make progress, or only to avoid thinking about the
   route?
3. Can switching, hazards, status, lock-in, recharge, sleep turns, or a sack
   make the boss spend scarce PP first?
4. Does the boss have enough recovery PP to erase the current damage plan?
5. Is our answer still an answer after spending its last phaze, recovery, Haze,
   or coverage PP?
6. What line wins once the target move reaches zero?

Decision rule:

- Conserve scarce PP when the move is the only remaining answer to a live route
  and the current use does not change the route.
- Spend scarce PP when not spending it lets the opponent create an irreversible
  route, or when the use forces the opponent's scarcer PP, item, Rest, or
  recovery turn.
- Attack or pivot instead of repeating recovery when the opponent's PP pool is
  larger and the loop is losing.
- In boss fights, check local PP and item data before calling a PP plan real.

- Lance, Clair, Red, and other setup-heavy fights can make phazing or Haze PP
  irreplaceable; spending it before the setup route is live can lose later.
- Koga, Brock, and Will can make Rapid Spin or hazard PP matter, but spin PP is
  only the bottleneck when hazards are the route being won or lost.

## Recipe: Endgame Forced-Line Audit

Trigger:

- The battle is down to a few relevant pieces, or one side's remaining route is
  mostly forced by HP, PP, status, hazards, weather, recovery, lock-in moves, or
  priority.

Rule:

- Stop evaluating moves by general value. In the endgame, write the shortest
  route tree that still covers legal branches. The best move is the one that
  keeps the winning forced line intact or breaks the opponent's forced line.

Source basis:

- Smogon's win-condition guide frames endgames around knowing what can win
  after blockers are removed.
- Smogon's risk/reward guide says to compare the likely move with the
  worst-case move and adjust risk based on matchup advantage.
- Smogon's GSC Spikes guide explains that hazards can decide slow endgames by
  making endless switching costly and turning the position into a PP war, but
  only if the user's own side can avoid the same switch tax.
- Reviewed GSC games show endgames often resolve through clocks: poison,
  Spikes, Rest cycles, recoil, Sleep Talk branches, phazing, and Explosion
  material rather than raw one-turn damage.

Audit:

1. List remaining pieces that can still change the result.
2. Mark each as `converter`, `blocker`, `sack`, or `spent`.
3. Count active clocks: poison, burn, weather, Perish Song, Leech Seed, Spikes,
   recoil, sleep turns, recovery PP, phazing PP, priority range, and lock turns.
4. For each serious move, write the next two turns if it works and if the
   opponent chooses the best legal punish.
5. Check whether the move creates a forced KO, forced Rest, forced switch,
   safe entry, PP win, or unavoidable clock.
6. If no forced line exists, choose the move that preserves the most live
   branches and avoids the irreversible loss.

Decision rule:

- Direct damage rises when it creates the next forced KO or denies recovery.
- Recovery rises when the healed piece is still the blocker or converter in the
  forced line.
- Sacrifice rises when the next entry creates a forced win and the sack no
  longer blocks a route.
- Waiting rises only when the opponent is already losing to an external clock
  and cannot create a new route during the waiting turn.
- Switching rises in PP endgames only when our side can switch without paying a
  worse clock than the opponent, or when the switch forces the opponent to spend
  the scarce PP first.
- Risky prediction rises only when safe play loses the forced-line race.

Failure signs:

- Trying to preserve all remaining Pokemon when one clean sack wins.
- Attacking for damage that does not change a KO, Rest, hazard, or priority
  threshold.
- Recovering a piece whose role is already over while the opponent sets up.
- Forgetting that priority, weather, recoil, poison, or hazards decide the last
  exchange before ordinary Speed or type advantage does.
- Treating a Rest, Sleep Talk, phaze, or PP branch as flavor instead of a
  possible route flip.

## Recipe: Long-Term Plan Revision

Trigger:

- A move misses, sleep lands or fails, a Pokemon wakes, a switch changes the
  matchup, a new move is revealed, damage contradicts expectations, or a crit
  changes a threshold.

Rule:

- Stop following the old script and re-score the public state.

Checklist:

1. What route did the previous plan depend on?
2. Which assumption changed?
3. Which pieces are now irreplaceable or expendable?
4. Did either side gain a new immediate punish?
5. Is the old route still live, worse, or dead?
6. What move now improves the best live route?
